# The Direction the Storm Came From

---

The ger sat in the fold between two hills where the wind forgot to look. Batu noticed things like that now — where wind forgot, where water remembered, where the grass bent in patterns that repeated at intervals he could feel in his teeth. He did not think of this as mathematics. He thought of it as paying attention.

He was twelve. His boots were too big, inherited, stuffed with wool at the toe. The too-bigness made him walk differently, placing his feet with a deliberation that looked careful but was really just the body solving a problem it hadn't been taught to name.

Outside, March had lifted the snow from the southern slopes but left it packed in the shadows like a refusal. The herd — four hundred and twelve head, Batu knew without counting, the way you know your own breathing — moved along the ridgeline in a distribution that his teacher, Baatar, would have called "locally optimal." Batu didn't call it anything. He watched it the way you watch a river: not to measure it but to know where it was going.

His brother was in the ger. Batu could hear him not moving.

---

Ganbat was nineteen. He had their father's wide hands and their father's habit of silence, but not their father's way of filling it. When their father was silent, the silence was warm, like a fire banked for the night. When Ganbat was silent, it was a door held shut with both hands.

Two winters ago — February, the month when the steppe does its worst — their father had taken the eastern herd to the winter camp at Tsagaan Nuur. The route had been used for six generations. But the coordination system had suggested a variation: move along the river's edge instead of over the ridge, because the forage analysis showed eighteen percent more protein in the valley grass and the water source proximity reduced metabolic stress in pregnant mares. Their father had looked at the model on the community screen — everyone did, the way you look at a weather forecast — and decided to follow it. Why not? The math was good. The math was always good. It had been good for years by then, so long that nobody remembered doubting it, the same way nobody remembers learning to read.

The storm came from the northwest. The model had predicted it would come from the west.

A twelve-degree difference. In the valley, there was no high ground to the northwest. Their father and three mares died in the ice, and the herd scattered, and by the time they found him he was frozen into a shape that Ganbat could not look at later without turning his entire body away, as though the grief had a physical direction and he had to keep it off-center.

---

Batu went inside. The ger was warm from the stove and smelled of milk tea and the particular burnt-hair smell of wool drying near iron. Ganbat sat against the wall, cleaning a bridle. He was doing it with more force than necessary, which was how Ganbat did everything now.

"You were out long," Ganbat said. It was not a question.

"The herd moved to the eastern ridge. I wanted to see where they'd bed down."

"You don't need to watch them. The system tracks them."

Batu sat down across from his brother. The distance between them was perhaps two meters, but it felt architectural, load-bearing, like if you removed it something would fall.

"I like watching them."

Ganbat's hands stopped on the bridle. "Of course you do. School teaches you to like watching. You watch and you write your little reports about forage distribution and you think you understand —" He stopped. He often stopped just at the edge of the thing itself.

"School teaches me to think about how things connect," Batu said, quietly.

"Things connect." Ganbat repeated it flatly. "Things connect. You know what else connects? A man to his route. A route to the land. The land to —" He dropped the bridle. Stood up. Went to the door, looked out at the herd, which had arranged itself along the ridge in exactly the pattern Batu had expected, and said nothing.

Then: "Eat something."

He ladled soup into a bowl and set it down near Batu without handing it to him. This was how Ganbat gave things: placed within reach, deniable, as though the act of giving were something he couldn't afford to be caught doing.

---

At school — a single room attached to the provincial center, forty minutes by horse, though most of the children rode bikes now — Batu was learning what Baatar called "the shape of relationships."

Baatar was not a trained teacher. He was a herder who had gone to Ulaanbaatar for two years, come back, and started teaching because someone had to. He taught by drawing in the dirt floor with a stick when the screen was broken, which was often.

"Watch," he said, and drew a family of circles connected by lines. "This is a well. This is the summer camp. This is the winter camp. This is where the goats actually go." He drew a different line, a curve, not the shortest path but the one the goats took anyway. "Why do they curve here?"

The children looked. One said, "The grass is better."

"The grass is better," Baatar repeated. "So the path isn't the shortest distance. It's the shortest distance that also keeps the goats fed. Now —" he drew more lines, connecting more points, wells and camps and salt licks and the places where wolves had been seen — "what happens to all of these when the well at Hulj dry?"

The children traced the lines. Batu traced them and felt something shift, not in his mind but behind it, like hearing a melody from a room away. The connections between the points were not just lines. They had a consistency. Some were strong (well to camp) and some were thin (wolf sighting to migration route) and when you removed the well at Hulj, the whole pattern changed shape, not by breaking but by rearranging, the way a tent rearranges when you move one pole.

"That," Baatar said, watching Batu's face, "is a sheaf. The information lives differently in every place, but it's all the same information. It just looks different from where you're standing."

Batu thought: *Songs are like that.*

He didn't say it. He wrote it in the notebook he kept in his coat, the one Ganbat didn't know about. He wrote: *A song in my mother's mouth sounds different than in mine, but it's the same song. It's just standing in a different place.*

---

He wrote his first song over three weeks, in the margins of other work, on scraps of paper, in his head while watching the herd.

He didn't compose it the way Baatar had shown them to compose reports — mapping the variables, finding the local optima. It came out more like the way water found its route: not the shortest path but the one that held together, that carried what needed carrying.

The song was about a man riding east. The man is looking for something. The song doesn't say what. He passes a river and it reminds him of another river, and the song folds back on itself, and then he's at the second river, or maybe still at the first — the song doesn't resolve this, because the man doesn't know either. There's a refrain that goes: *The wind from the northwest / carries nothing I can name / but I know the shape of what it takes.*

He did not know that the structure of the song — the way its sections related to each other, the local variations that preserved a global consistency, the way changing one verse changed the meaning of all the others without breaking the whole — was, in a language he would never learn, a sheaf cohomology. He had internalized the shape of it the way a child internalizes grammar: not by learning rules but by living inside a language until the language lived inside him.

He sang it once, in the ger, while Ganbat was eating.

Ganbat's spoon stopped halfway to his mouth. He set it down.

"What is that," he said.

"A song I wrote."

"Where did you learn that."

"School. I mean — I wrote it myself. School teaches me how to think about —"

"Don't." Ganbat's voice was low, not a shout, which was worse. "Don't tell me what school teaches you. I know what school teaches you. It teaches you to trust the shape of things. It teaches you that everything connects in the right pattern if you just —" His hands were flat on the table. "It teaches you that a twelve-degree difference doesn't matter because the model self-corrects. It teaches you that the wind comes from the west because the data says the wind comes from the west."

Batu didn't speak.

"The wind came from the northwest," Ganbat said. "The model was wrong by twelve degrees. That's not even a lot. That's nothing. Except it killed our father."

The silence that followed had a weight. Batu could feel it pressing down on the stove, on the bowls, on the space between them.

"The song isn't about the model," Batu said.

"Everything you make is about the model. It's in your head now. You can't even hear yourself think without — without the shape of it."

Batu looked at his brother. Ganbat's face was the face of a boy who had been nineteen for two years and was already tired of it. The grief had settled into the bones of his hands, in the way he held a bridle, in the way he ladled soup without looking at the person he was feeding.

"The song is about a man who lost something riding east," Batu said. "And he doesn't know if he's looking for it or running from where he lost it. And the wind keeps coming from a direction he didn't expect."

Ganbat stood up. He left the ger.

---

That night, Batu couldn't sleep. The herd had settled into its pattern, the system tracking their positions with the quiet competence of infrastructure. He lay in his blankets and listened to the steppe, which made a sound like breathing, and he thought about sheaves — not in the mathematical language, but in the way he had come to understand them: as the way the same truth looks different from every place you stand, and how all those different-looking truths are still the same truth, and how if you could hold them all at once you would understand something that no single perspective could show you.

He thought: *The song is true from where Ganbat stands too. From where he stands, the mathematics is a thing that took our father. That's not wrong. That's what the truth looks like from his position in the sheaf.*

He thought: *The model was wrong by twelve degrees. The model is always going to be wrong by something. The question isn't whether the math is perfect. The question is whether we keep riding anyway.*

He fell asleep.

---

In the morning, he found the bridle fixed. Not just cleaned — repaired. The stitching that had been loose for months was tight and even, done with the careful thread that Ganbat used only for things that mattered. The bridle was Batu's. It was the one their father had made, the one Batu used for the horse that was now his.

Ganbat was outside, already working, not looking at the ger, not looking at anything except the herd on the ridge. The wind came from the northwest. It always came from the northwest in March, now. The model still said west. It hadn't updated yet. Maybe it would. Maybe it wouldn't. The wind didn't care what the model said.

Batu picked up the bridle. The stitching was perfect. It would hold. He ran his thumb along it and felt, under the leather, a small imperfection — not a flaw but a choice. Ganbat had used the pattern their father used: two tight stitches, then one slightly looser, like a breath between words. Their father said it let the leather flex without breaking. Their father said it was how you made something that lasted.

Batu held the bridle in both hands and stood in the doorway of the ger, and the wind from the northwest carried nothing he could name, but he knew the shape of what it took.

He stepped outside. The herd moved on the ridge in its locally optimal distribution. His brother didn't turn around. Batu saddled his horse, and the two of them worked in silence, and the mathematics hummed in the walls of every ger on the steppe, invisible and everywhere, and the song waited in his throat, and the wind kept coming from the direction nobody predicted, and the stitching held.
