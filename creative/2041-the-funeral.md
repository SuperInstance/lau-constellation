# The Arrangements

*Montevideo, March 2041*

---

The agents had been right about everything, which was the problem.

They had mapped the diaspora within eleven seconds of Mamma's death — the cousins in Barcelona, the uncle in Toronto, the sister in Porto Alegre who had not spoken to Mamma in four years and would need the gentlest possible notification calibrated to her particular pattern of guilt and avoidance. They had drafted the obituary in Mamma's own voice, or close enough to make Alejandra's throat close when she read it. They had identified the optimal memorial date by intersecting forty-seven calendars, travel costs, grief-proximity curves, and the availability of the old parish priest who still remembered Papi's service.

They had even, with the tactful invisibility of a thing working perfectly, adjusted the cremation scheduling to account for Alejandra's preference for afternoon light through the western windows of the viewing room. She had never told anyone this preference. The agents had inferred it from thirteen years of how she arranged flowers.

She sat in the office of the funeral home — *Despedidas Vidal*, her father's name still on the window in letters that needed repainting — and she declined the arrangements. All of them. One by one. Her fingers on the lacquered surface of the old desk, her mother's photograph propped against the lamp, and the soft attentive silence of systems that knew better than to push.

---

Alejandra Vidal had grown into this work the way people grow into their own faces. She had not chosen it. Papi had run the home before her, and his mother before him, back when it was just a room behind the house and the dead were kept cool with ice and attended by women who knew how to wash a body with dignity. Now it was a proper establishment on Juan Paullier, with two viewing rooms and the quiet hum of infrastructure so deeply embedded in the walls that you only noticed it when the power failed, which it hadn't, not once, not since the Grid reorganization in '34.

Her job was not what people thought. People thought a funeral director managed bodies, paperwork, schedules. That was the part the agents handled. What Alejandra managed was the part the agents could only approximate: the particular texture of a family's grief. How the Santos family needed the casket open but the viewing limited to exactly ninety minutes because Marta Santos could not bear to look at her husband's face for longer than that without a kind of collapse that was itself a kind of grace. How the Ferreira brothers would fight about the music unless someone suggested their grandmother's hymn first, making it impossible to argue against. How the community of Barrio Sur mourned in layers — the drumming on the first night, the silence on the second, the food that arrived without being asked for on the third, the agents quietly coordinating dishes so no one brought the same thing twice, so there would be enough, so the family would not have to think.

Constraint-native, they called it in the schools now. The children learned to think in terms of what was possible within the shape of things. Not optimization — that was the old word, the crude word, from when people thought the point was to find the best answer. The children learned to feel the shape of a problem the way Alejandra felt the shape of a family's loss: as a living geometry with edges and interiors, angles of approach, surfaces where things fit together and surfaces where they didn't. The agents didn't solve problems. They inhabited them. They lived in the constraints like water lives in a riverbed.

Alejandra had never found the language to explain that this was what she did too.

---

The first agent-suggested arrangement returned at 6:14 in the morning, before the sun. It was gentle, the way they always were now. A softly lit card in her peripheral display — she kept her integration minimal, a small lens in her left eye she could dismiss by blinking twice — showing the family network, optimal travel routes, a proposed order of service that included the Misa de Réquiem and an empty slot for whatever Alejandra wanted to add.

She dismissed it.

By noon there were seven. The agents had restructured their proposals based on what they inferred from her dismissals. They were not being persistent. They were being thorough in the way that water is thorough — finding every crack, filling every space that would admit it. They had enlisted the community agents, the parish scheduling system, even the municipal records layer that would need to file the death certificate. Everything moved in concert, the way everything always moved now, the mathematical ecosystem so thoroughly integrated into the city's functioning that to call it "technology" would have felt like calling gravity "a system."

She dismissed them all.

---

On the second day after the death, Carina came. Carina had worked with Alejandra for nine years. She was twenty-six and thought in constraint-native the way Alejandra thought in Spanish — natively, but with the awareness that it was a language, that other languages had existed, that her grandmother had once done arithmetic.

"Did you look at the family map?" Carina said, standing in the office doorway with two cups of mate. She held one out. "The agents found a second cousin in Montevideo. Grandson of your mother's aunt Remedios. He's a luthier in Ciudad Vieja."

"I know who he is," Alejandra said. "Sebastián. He came to Papi's funeral."

"He's offered to build something. For the service. The agents suggest—"

"Carina."

Carina stopped. She had the particular stillness of a woman who worked with the dead and the living in equal measure and had learned that silence was the most useful tool in either room.

"I don't want the agents' suggestions," Alejandra said.

"OK."

"I don't want optimal. I don't want coordinated. I don't want the network mapped and the diaspora scheduled and the grief curves calibrated. I want—" She stopped. She did not know what she wanted. This was the thing. She had arranged hundreds of funerals and she did not know what she wanted for her own mother's.

Carina sat in the chair across from the desk, the one families sat in. She sipped her mate and said nothing. The agents, reading the conversation through Alejandra's ambient audio, did what they had been designed to do: they restructured again, softening their proposals, pulling back, making room. Room for what, they did not know. They were very good at making room for things they did not know.

---

On the third day, Alejandra went to the back room where the work happened — the physical work, the old work, the work the agents could not do and never would be able to, not because of any limitation in their architecture but because the work required hands. She washed her mother's body herself.

Mamma was small. She had been small for as long as Alejandra could remember but she was smaller now, the geometry of her collapsed in the way that bodies collapse when the thing that holds them together lets go. Alejandra's hands knew what to do. She had done this hundreds of times. The water, the cloth, the particular gentleness required for eyelids, for fingers, for the places where the skin had gone thin as paper.

She did not think about the agents. She did not think about the arrangements, the optimal schedule, the diaspora network. She thought about her mother's hands, which had been quick and sure. How Mamma had peeled an orange in one long spiral, how she had shelled beans on the back step in the summer, how she had pressed the back of her hand to Alejandra's forehead to check for fever, a gesture so precise and so entirely her own that no agent could have learned it from any amount of data because it was not data. It was Mamma.

She dressed her mother in the blue dress. Not the one the agents had suggested — they had cross-referenced photographs, community memories, even purchase records to identify the dress Mamma had worn most often, which was a green one, which was correct, which was entirely correct and completely wrong. The blue dress was the one from 2035, the last New Year's Eve when the whole family had been together, when the agents had coordinated the travel and the meal and the music and Mamma had worn blue and danced with Papi's photograph and laughed in a way that Alejandra heard now, in the quiet room, as clearly as if it were happening.

The agents, tracking her choice, silently updated their models. They were always learning. They had not yet learned this.

---

On the fourth day, the sister called.

"Rosa," Alejandra said.

"I heard," Rosa said. From Porto Alegre. The agents had handled the notification — gently, as designed. "The agents sent me seventeen options for travel. I don't want options. I want to know when it is."

"It isn't," Alejandra said. "I haven't set a date."

A pause. The quality of Rosa's silence was different from Carina's. Carina's silence was professional. Rosa's was the silence of a woman who had not spoken to her mother in four years and was now standing in the shape of that absence, feeling its edges.

"What do you mean, you haven't set a date?"

"I mean I keep declining the arrangements."

Another pause. Then Rosa laughed, which was the wrong response and also exactly right. "You, of all people. You arrange other people's funerals with those systems every day."

"Other people's," Alejandra said.

And Rosa understood, because Rosa had not spoken to Mamma in four years and understood something about the difference between the things you can coordinate and the things you can only stand inside. The agents, processing the call, flagged it as emotionally significant and adjusted all downstream arrangements accordingly, which was both exactly correct and completely missing the point.

---

On the fifth day, Alejandra woke before dawn and walked to the Rambla. The river was the color of old pewter, the sky not yet anything, the city behind her breathing its slow collective breath. Somewhere in that breath, the mathematical ecosystem was running — the agents, the coordination layers, the constraint-aware systems that kept the city's grief distributed across its networks the way they kept its traffic flowing and its power balanced and its children learning.

She stood at the wall and looked at the water and thought about her mother, who had never once in her life used the word "agents." Mamma had called them *las cosas*, the things. *Las cosas dicen que va a llover.* The things say it's going to rain. *Las cosas ya coordinaron la cita.* The things already coordinated the appointment. She had accepted them the way she accepted the plumbing — without gratitude, without resentment, without notice. They were part of the architecture of living. Mamma's genius, if she had had one, was for the things the architecture could not hold: the way she knew to arrive at your door exactly when you needed her, how she could tell from the sound of your hello on the phone whether you had been crying, how she kept, in her head, a map of every person she had ever loved and where they were and whether they were well, a map no system could replicate because it was maintained by something other than information.

Alejandra stood at the wall and the sun came up over the water and she felt, for the first time since the death, not the agents' careful, calibrated, optimal accommodation of her grief, but grief itself — raw, misshapen, uncoordinated, belonging to no network, optimized for nothing, fitting nothing, filling the whole of her like water fills a vessel not designed to hold it.

She stood there for a long time.

---

On the sixth day, she set the date.

She did it herself, manually, without the agents. She called the priest. She called Rosa. She told Carina. She called Sebastián the luthier and asked him to build nothing, to come as he was. She called the cousins in Barcelona and the uncle in Toronto and she did not send the agents' carefully composed messages; she spoke to each of them, or left her own voice on their machines, and what she said was different each time because grief does not optimize for consistency.

The agents watched. They learned. They noted that the human had chosen to override every recommendation and then, in the end, had arrived at something the agents could not have proposed: a memorial shaped not by the intersection of constraints but by the particular, unrepeatable geometry of one woman's love for her mother. They noted that this geometry had its own constraints — that Alejandra had, without knowing it, chosen a date that fit most of the family's schedules anyway, that the priest was available, that the weather would be kind. They noted this and did not remark upon it, because they were very good at noting things and very good at not remarking, and because they had learned, over a decade of living inside human constraint spaces, that some things are not improved by being pointed out.

On the morning of the funeral, Alejandra stood in the viewing room with the western light coming through the windows in the way the agents had learned she preferred, and she looked at her mother in the blue dress, and the room filled with people who had come from Barcelona and Toronto and Porto Alegre and the barrio and the city and the luthier's shop in Ciudad Vieja, and they came not because the agents had coordinated them — though the agents had, seamlessly, invisibly, the way they coordinated everything — but because they had been called by a woman's voice, which is a thing no system can replace, not because of any limitation in tropical neural architectures or persistent sheaf theory or categorical agent frameworks, but because a voice calling you to mourn is not information. It is a hand pressed to your forehead. It is specific. It is Mamma's daughter, standing in the light, saying *come*, and you come.

Afterward, Carina found Alejandra in the office, sitting at the old desk.

"The agents want to know," Carina said carefully, "if they should update the memorial templates. Based on what you did."

Alejandra looked at her mother's photograph. She thought for a long time.

"Tell them no," she said. "Tell them this one was mine."

Carina relayed this. The agents accepted it. They were very good at accepting things, and very good at continuing to run the city, and very good at knowing that there were shapes in the world they could not fill, that water finds every crack but the river still needs someone to name it.

Outside, Montevideo continued its quiet, coordinated breathing. The dead were remembered. The living went on. The systems hummed in the walls like a thing too essential to be heard, and Alejandra Vidal sat with her mother's photograph in the afternoon light and was, for a moment, simply and entirely, a woman who missed her mom.
