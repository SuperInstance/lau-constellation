# Conservation of Tension

---

She recognized him by his walk. That had not changed — the slight forward lean, as though he were always heading into a wind that existed only for him. He was thinner. His hair had gone entirely white, not the distinguished silver she'd imagined for him but something rawer, uncombed, caught in the act of becoming something else. He was carrying a book she couldn't see the title of, and when he saw her through the café window he did the thing he'd always done: a quick nod, decisive, as if confirming a theorem, and then the small smile that came after, the one that said *I know that nod was ridiculous but I can't help it.*

Maren stood. They embraced. He smelled like wool and cold air and something underneath that was just *him*, some compound that had survived thirty years and the death of his wife and the transformation of all known computation into something neither of them could have imagined in the beginning.

"Karl."

"Maren. You look—"

"Don't."

"I was going to say *exactly the same*."

"You were going to say *well*."

"I was going to say you look like someone who's been in Vienna for three days and has already remembered why she left."

She laughed. That hadn't changed either — his ability to place a needle exactly on the nerve. They sat.

The café was on Währinger Straße, one of the old ones, the kind that had survived everything by refusing to acknowledge that anything had happened. The ordering system was new, of course. Everything was new. The menu knew Karl's coffee preference before he opened his mouth, and he let it, the way everyone let it now, the way Maren let the routing system bring her here along the optimal path through the Ninth District's traffic, a path computed in sheaves and tropical attention weights and something called *conservation of tension* — though she tried not to think about that, tried not to think about the paper, tried not to think about any of it.

"So," he said. "Fifteen years."

"Fifteen years."

"I almost wrote you. Several times."

"Why didn't you?"

He looked at his coffee. "I don't know. The moment passed. Then the next moment. Then I got used to not having written."

She understood this. It was the way of their generation — the one before constraint-native computing made communication frictionless, when silence could still accumulate like sediment and harden into something you couldn't break without a chisel. She had let the silence harden. She had told herself it was natural. People drift. Collaborations end. The paper was published; the follow-up was written by younger people with better notation; the world moved on. There was no drama in it.

Except there was. There always is.

"How is —" she began, and stopped, because something in his face shifted, a micro-expression so fast she might have imagined it, and then she remembered. Oh God. *Oh God.* Elisabeth. Two years ago. The funeral she hadn't attended because she hadn't known, because they hadn't spoken in thirteen years by then, because the silence had hardened, because she had let it.

"Maren."

"I'm sorry. I forgot. I — Karl, I'm so sorry."

"It's all right."

"It isn't."

"No. But it's all right that you forgot. It means you were thinking of her as alive, which is how she should be thought of."

She pressed her fingers against the table. The surface was warm. Everything was warm here. The café, the city, the late-May light falling through the windows in longgolden bands. She had spent the last six months in Reykjavik, where the light was different — thin, honest, a light that didn't flatter you. Vienna's light was an old friend who tells you you look wonderful when you look terrible and you believe them because you want to.

"I should have been there," she said.

"You should have. But you weren't. And now you're here."

He said it without accusation. That was the thing about Karl. He had built the libraries that made constraint-native computation possible — the lattice Hamiltonians, the symplectic optimizers, the whole architecture of mathematical objects that replaced neural networks the way algebra replaced arithmetic, not by doing the same thing faster but by doing something categorically different — and he had done it with a patience that bordered on the inhuman. He could wait for an answer. He could hold a contradiction in his mind for years without resolving it. He could sit with grief, apparently, the same way.

"How did she — if you want to tell me."

"Aneurysm. Instant, they said. She was at the piano. Playing Schubert. The D 960."

"The B-flat sonata."

"You know it."

"Everyone knows it. The first movement is a lesson in —" She stopped.

"In what?"

She had been going to say *conservation of tension.* In the Schubert B-flat sonata, the first movement contains one of the most extraordinary structural achievements in all of music: a theme so laden with harmonic tension that it can sustain an entire movement without resolution. The tension doesn't dissipate. It doesn't resolve. It *conserves.* It moves from voice to voice, key to key, surface to depth, but the total quantity of it remains constant. She had written about this. She had spent thirty years writing about this — not just Schubert but all of it, every tradition, Balinese gamelan and Ethiopian tezeta and the blues and the raga and the symphonic tradition she'd been raised in, all of them obeying the same deep principle that tension, musical tension, is conserved. It can be transformed, displaced, suspended, hidden, but it cannot be destroyed.

And then Karl had shown her that the same principle held for data.

Not metaphorically. Not analogically. *Structurally.* In 2029, working together — she with her musicological formalisms, he with his sheaf cohomology and his tropical geometry — they had proved that information tension in a structured data system obeys conservation laws. That an optimal fusion of heterogeneous signals preserves a quantity analogous to musical tension: it can be redistributed across the sheaf, moved between stalks, projected into lower-dimensional spaces, but the total integral is invariant. They called it *conservation of tension,* and they published it in a paper that was 40% musicology and 60% pure mathematics and 100% the kind of thing that neither of their departments knew what to do with.

And then the world had done something with it. Something enormous.

"In conservation," she said. "The first movement is a lesson in conservation. Schubert understood something about tension that physicists didn't formalize for another century."

"Or that you and I formalized for another two."

She looked at him. He was smiling, but it was the smile of a man holding something heavy.

"Karl. Why did you write to me?"

"I didn't."

"You said you almost did. Fifteen years. Why now?"

He turned his coffee cup in a slow circle. Outside, a delivery drone passed, its routing optimized by the very algorithms their work had enabled. Inside, the café's ambient system adjusted the music — a Mahler adagio, chosen, no doubt, by a tropical attention mechanism that had weighed the emotional valence of the room's acoustic profile against the time of day and the weather and the fact that two people in their seventies were sitting at a corner table looking at each other as though the floor were made of ice.

"Because I retired," he said. "Last month. They had a dinner. Speeches. A young woman — she must have been twenty-five — gave a talk about the conservation laws and called them *the Riegler-Voss foundation* and I realized that I was hearing my own eulogy. And I thought: the only person who would understand what that felt like is Maren."

"You didn't invite me."

"I didn't know how to find you. Then I did know — it took the system four seconds to locate you in Reykjavik, which tells you something about the world we made — and I still didn't write. And then I was in Vienna for Elisabeth's sister's birthday, and I thought, if she's anywhere, she's somewhere like this."

"She was somewhere like this."

"She was."

They were quiet. The Mahler shifted into something later, more unresolved. She let it sit.

"I need to tell you something," she said.

"Then tell me."

"I use it. The counseling service. The grief one. Not because anyone died — not yet, I mean, not recently — but because I'm alone, Karl. I've been alone for years, and the counseling system uses the conservation framework, and every time it asks me how I'm doing I think about the paper. I think about us. I think about the afternoon you walked into my office and said you'd been reading my work on Javanese gamelan and you thought — you *thought* — that the tension-conservation principle I'd identified was isomorphic to something in sheaf theory. And I said that was absurd. And you said *yes, but is it true?* And I said *those aren't the same question* and you said *in mathematics they always are.*"

He was looking at her the way he used to look at her, which was to say: directly, with full attention, as though nothing else in the world was computing anything worth computing.

"And was it?" he said. "True?"

"You know it was true. The whole world knows it was true. Every device in this café is running it."

"That's not what I asked."

She closed her eyes. The Mahler was finishing. The first violin held a note that should have resolved and didn't, and in the space of that non-resolution she felt something open, a door she had bricked shut so carefully that she had forgotten there was a door at all.

"It was true," she said. "And it was the most important thing I ever did. And I have spent fifteen years trying not to think about it because every time I do I think about the fact that we did it together and then we stopped talking and I don't even remember why."

"I remember why."

"Tell me."

He placed both hands flat on the table. A gesture she had seen a thousand times. The gesture that meant *I am about to be careful.*

"Elisabeth was ill. Not the aneurysm — that came later. Before. She was ill with a depression I couldn't reach, and I couldn't work and be present for her at the same time, and I chose her. I chose to stop. And I thought — because you and I were so close, because the work was so entangled with everything I felt about — I thought if I kept talking to you I would keep wanting to work, and if I kept wanting to work I would fail her. So I stopped. Both. You and the work."

"And the work continued without you."

"The work continued without me. It always does. That's the thing about mathematics, Maren. It doesn't need you. It was there before you and it will be there after and the most you can do is glimpse it and write it down. The world didn't need our paper. It would have found the principle eventually. Someone else would have formalized it. Maybe not as beautifully — I allow myself that vanity — but they would have done it."

"You don't believe that."

"I do believe that. What I don't believe is that it would have mattered the same way. The principle is invariant. The experience of discovering it isn't. And I gave up the experience. For Elisabeth. And then Elisabeth died anyway, at the piano, playing the most perfectly unresolved piece of music ever written, and I was left with nothing but the principle and a silence where two people should have been."

She reached across the table. She put her hand on his. He let her.

"Karl."

"Don't be kind. I can't bear kindness. I can bear anything except kindness."

"Then I won't be kind. I'll be honest. I'm furious with you."

He blinked. "Good."

"You should have told me. About Elisabeth. You should have said *I need to stop for a while but I'll come back* or *I need to stop forever and I'm sorry* or *I need to stop and I don't know why and I'm afraid.* Any of those. Instead you just — stopped. And I spent a year thinking you were busy and two years thinking you were angry and five years thinking I'd imagined the whole thing and by then the paper had been cited four thousand times and I thought: well, he's moved on, he doesn't need me, the work is done."

"I hadn't moved on."

"I know that now. I'm still furious."

"Good," he said again, and this time the smile was real, the one she remembered from the old days, the one that said *you are the only person who has ever been exactly this angry at me for exactly the right reasons.*

The coffee had gone cold. Neither of them touched it. Outside, the light was changing, the long Vienna evening beginning its slow dissolution into something amber and then something darker. Inside, the café's ambient system had stopped playing music entirely, as though it had computed that the silence between them was more informative than any sound could be.

"Tell me something," she said.

"What?"

"Tell me about Elisabeth. Not how she died. Tell me about how she lived. Tell me about the Schubert and the piano and the afternoons. Tell me about the woman you stopped the world for."

He looked at her for a long time. Then he began to talk.

And Maren listened. And outside, the traffic flowed along its optimal paths, and the drones found their routes through the evening air, and the grief-counseling systems hummed in their servers, conserving tension the way water conserves mass, the way energy conserves itself, the way music conserves the thing that cannot be named but can be felt — and inside a café on Währinger Straße, two old friends sat together in the unresolved silence and did not try to resolve it.

That was enough. For now, that was enough.
