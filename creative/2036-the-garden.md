# The Garden at the End of Everything

The argument started, as most of their arguments did now, in the garden.

From the back porch of the farmhouse on Lincoln Hill Road, Ruth could see the whole thing: the raised beds her husband had built in 1998, the hoop house he'd never quite finished, the compost bins that had been full to bursting every spring for thirty-seven years. The garden was his domain. Had always been. But that was before the arthritis settled into his knuckles like a punishment, and before the house got its agent.

"Tobacco," he said, the word landing like a stone.

She looked up from her tea. "Tobacco? What on earth for?"

"To kill the aphids." Francis was on the garden path, the morning sun catching his white hair. "My grandfather used to grow a row of it around the tomatoes. The nicotine gets in the soil. Pests hate it."

"We're not growing tobacco, Frank. I can barely get you to take your blood pressure medication."

"I'm not suggesting we smoke it." His voice had that edge it got when he felt patronized. "It's a companion plant. It's permaculture."

"It is not permaculture. It's a weird hill to die on."

She could have let it go then. But they were both, at seventy-three and seventy-one, terrible at letting things go. They came from families where a disagreement was a form of conversation, and the decade difference between them meant they'd had forty-six years of practice at this particular sport.

"You know," she said, setting down her mug, "the house has an optimization for the watering schedule. The Wasserstein module. It's been analyzing soil moisture since April. If you'd let it handle the garden—"

The silence was immediate and total.

"I've been doing it by hand since before you moved into this house," Francis said. "My grandfather grew tomatoes in this soil without any agents, without any—" he gestured vaguely at the house, at the air, at the invisible mathematics running through their walls "—systems. And they were fine."

"They were fine. That's your standard? Fine?"

"They tasted good."

"They tasted fine," she said. "The ones from last September, the ones the agent timed the watering around the heat wave? Those tasted like something else entirely. You said so yourself. You said they were the best tomatoes you'd ever grown."

"People say things."

"Frank, you cried."

He picked up the trowel and did not look at her. "Don't be ridiculous."

"You cried," she repeated. "I was there. I handed you the handkerchief. You told me they reminded you of the ones your grandfather grew when you were eight, but better. You said the word better. I remember."

This was the shape of modern arguments between two people who had been married long enough to remember everything. Every word. Every contradiction. Every time you had changed your mind.

He set the trowel down and straightened with a wince. "It's not about the tomatoes."

"Oh, I know." She didn't say I told you so. She didn't need to. The words hung in the air between them anyway.

"The garden is mine," he said, and there it was. The real thing. "The house is yours. The computer stuff is yours. I don't tell you how to run your classes or your—your constraint whatever. And you don't get to tell me how to dig in the dirt I've been digging for fifty years."

She wanted to say: It's not about telling you how to dig. It's about your hands. It's about watching you struggle with the hose. It's about the fact that I love you and you're seventy-three years old and your father died of a heart attack at sixty-nine and I am not going to lose you to a garden.

But she didn't. She said, "The water optimization would use sixty percent less than your manual schedule. The house measured it. The soil sensors, the weather prediction—"

"Ruth."

"—the Wasserstein distance between the moisture profile and the ideal growing conditions is minimized over the whole growing season, Frank, that's not opinion, that's—"

"Ruth!"

She stopped.

"It's mine," he said, and his voice cracked on the last word, just slightly, just enough. "Everything else has changed. The way I write letters, the way I read the news, the way I talk to the doctor about my prescriptions. All of it is handled by something I don't understand. I get in the car and it knows where I'm going. I sit down to write a check and it reminds me of my balance. Every time I reach for something, it's already there, already figured out, already optimized. The garden is the one thing I still do with my hands. The one thing I still get wrong on my own."

She didn't have a response to that. She sat on the porch step and watched him turn back to the soil. The morning had moved into that hollow quiet of late spring in Vermont, the birds doing their work, the wind moving through the maples.

"I could tell it to stop," she said softly. "I could tell the house not to interfere."

"Can you?"

"Yes, Frank. I designed half of it. I can tell it to leave the garden to you."

He didn't respond for a long time. He knelt and worked the soil between his fingers, the way he always did, as though checking for something only his hands could find.

"That's not the same," he said finally.

"I know."

"If you tell it to stop, it's because I asked. It's still your choice."

"I know."

"Then what are we fighting about?"

He looked at her then, and she saw the tiredness in him that went deeper than the morning. "I don't know. What are we fighting about?"

"Oranges," she said. "It tastes like oranges, that last batch."

"What?"

"The tomatoes. They tasted like oranges. That's what you said when you cried. You said they tasted like the ones from 1967, from your grandfather's garden, but with oranges. You said you didn't know a tomato could taste like oranges."

He stared at her. "I don't remember saying that."

"I do."

He sat down heavily on the porch step beside her, close enough that their shoulders touched. They stayed like that for a while. The garden was doing what gardens do, which was to ignore the people arguing above it. The bees had their own agenda. The soil didn't care who won.

"I don't want to fight," he said.

"Neither do I."

"But I'm not wrong."

"You're not wrong," she agreed. "Neither am I."

"That's the problem with smart people marrying each other."

"We're both always right."

"It's exhausting."

The granddaughter came out through the screen door, letting it slam behind her. Mira was nineteen, home from college for three weeks before her summer internship in Burlington, and she had the particular talent of the young for walking into a room and knowing immediately what she was walking into.

"You two fighting about the garden again?"

"It's a philosophical discussion," Francis said.

"It's a disagreement about optimal resource allocation," Ruth said.

Mira sat down on the step below them, the way she'd done since she was tall enough to reach the ground from there. "Grandpa, I looked at the soil sensor data last night."

"You did?"

"I was bored. You left your tablet unlocked."

Ruth couldn't help the smile. "Smart girl."

"The thing is," Mira said, not looking at either of them, "the agent isn't watering the garden. It's been in passive mode the whole time. Reading only, no write access. Grandma set it up that way when the house was installed."

Ruth blinked. "I did?"

"Yeah. The log says: 'Garden zone — observation only, by operator preference.' The date on it is three years ago." Mira finally looked up. "The tomatoes from last September, the ones you're both taking credit for? The watering schedule was Grandpa's. All Grandpa's. The agent just watched."

Francis turned to look at Ruth.

She felt the heat rise to her face. "I genuinely forgot. I was so sure—"

"You were giving me credit for the agent's work," he said slowly. "And I was taking credit for my own, and fighting you about it."

"Yes."

"And the tomatoes were mine all along."

"Yes."

He was quiet for a moment. Then he started to laugh. It started low and built, the way his laugh always did, shaking his shoulders, and it was the first time she'd heard him laugh like that in weeks.

"Forty-six years," he said. "And you're still wrong."

"One time."

"You were spectacularly wrong."

"I was trying to love you."

"Oh, I know." He put his arm around her. "You do it in the most annoying possible way. I'd recognize it anywhere."

Mira stood up, brushing dirt off her jeans. "I'm going into town. You two want anything?"

"Air," Francis said. "Tobacco seeds. A time machine."

"I'll get coffee."

The screen door slammed again, and they were alone with the garden and the bees and the mild math of the spring afternoon. Overhead, a satellite nobody thought about anymore was routing data through a sheaf of collapsed information spaces, determining optimal bandwidth allocation for a farming cooperative in Mozambique. In the house behind them, the agent was recalculating its models against observation, flagging nothing, adjusting nothing, waiting for instructions that might never come.

"I have an idea," Ruth said.

"Oh God."

"Let the house handle exactly one bed. The one closest to the house, the one with the peppers. Just this season. If they taste worse, I admit defeat permanently."

"Permanently? You?"

"Permanently."

"And if they taste better?"

She took his hand. His knuckles were swollen, the way they always were in spring. She held it anyway, the way she always did.

"If they taste better," she said, "you still win. You can tell everyone you grew them. I won't correct you."

"That's not much of a bet."

"It's the best I've got."

He looked at the pepper bed, a rectangle of dirt that had given them exactly eight edible peppers last year. Then he looked at his hands. Then he looked at her.

"Fine," he said. "One bed. But I reserve the right to complain."

"You'd complain about winning."

"Probably."

She leaned her head against his shoulder. The afternoon stretched out in front of them. The house hummed its inaudible hum, doing its invisible work, running the quiet algebra of the world. And in the garden, the pepper bed waited to see which future would claim it — the old way or the new way, the hands or the math, the argument or the answer neither of them was quite ready to admit they'd already reached.
