# The Song He Hasn't Reached Yet

---

The club doesn't have a name. Not really. The sign outside says **MUSIC** in neon that's been half-dead since 2031, and the 'M' flickers in a way that makes it look like **USIC** on humid nights, which is close enough to *music* if you squint and you've had two drinks. Marcus plays there Thursdays. He has played there Thursdays for three years, which is longer than his daughter has been alive, which is longer than he has had the constraint system, which is longer than he had his right hand.

The order of those things matters. He could tell you the order. He doesn't.

---

He sets up at six. The piano is a Yamaha from the '20s — the real kind, strings and felt, not the holographic junk they put in hotel lobbies now. Doc Kresge keeps it tuned because she believes in things. Marcus believes in things too, but different ones. He believes Thelonious Monk would have hated most of what gets called jazz in 2038, and he believes his daughter Amara is going to be taller than him by the time she's twelve, and he believes there is a song inside the space between his left hand and where his right hand used to be.

The constraint system lives in a band around his left wrist, matte black, thinner than a watch. Nobody asks about it anymore. Three years ago they asked. Now it's just part of the furniture, the way the cracked leather booth by the bathroom is part of the furniture, the way Marcus's missing hand is part of the furniture. Chicago is good at not looking twice.

He flexes his left hand over the keys. C-minor. Always C-minor to start. Not because the system needs a calibration — it doesn't, hasn't since the tropical attention lattice learned his tendons three years ago — but because C-minor is home. Because Thelonious played "Round Midnight" and the world shifted on its axis, and every jazz pianist since has been living in the ruins of that shift, building cathedrals out of the rubble.

His left hand drops.

---

Here is what happens when his left hand drops, what has been happening invisibly for three years:

The constraint-synthesis system reads the impulse patterns in his forearm — not the electrical signals, which are noisy and cheap, but the *geometric* signature of intent encoded in the muscle fiber deformations. A sheaf-theoretic model tracks the local constraints (finger position, velocity, the harmonic neighborhood implied by his chord voicings) and lifts them into a global space where the music *wants* to go. Tropical semirings handle the attention allocation — which voicings to complete, which to leave open, where the rhythm needs weight versus breath — using min-plus algebra that treats musical time as a conserved quantity the way physics treats energy. You cannot add beats. You can only distribute them.

None of this is interesting to Marcus.

What is interesting to Marcus is that when his left hand plays a C-minor triad in second inversion with a particular weight on the G, the system understands — has learned, over three years of listening to him listen to himself — that his right hand would have reached for the E-flat an octave up, would have hovered, would have *almost* played it, and then would have deflected to a D-natural at the last moment because Marcus Cole does not resolve when he can defer. The system knows this. It learned it from a thousand hours of Marcus playing alone in his apartment on Halsted Street, Amara asleep in the next room, the monitor picking up her breathing and the constraint lattice quietly incorporating even that rhythm into its model of what Marcus's music should sound like.

The right-hand part emerges from the piano's own speakers — close-miked, warm, indistinguishable from real strings being struck by real felt. The audience at the club cannot tell. Marcus can tell, but only in the way you can tell your own heartbeat: as background, as fact, as the machinery of being alive.

He plays.

---

He plays "Round Midnight" first, because you always play "Round Midnight" first if you have any respect for the form. His left hand does most of the work — walking bass, rolled tenths, the deep harmonic architecture that most pianists relegate to accompaniment. But for Marcus, the left hand *is* the architecture. The constraint system fills in the right-hand melody, and it fills it in the way Marcus would have: with hesitation, with silences that are almost more articulated than the notes, with that particular Chicagoan reluctance to show off even when showing off is the whole point.

The Thursday crowd is thin. A couple in the back booth, the woman's head on the man's shoulder, both of them twenty-two and beautiful in the way that twenty-two is beautiful, which is to say temporarily. Doc Kresge at the bar, drinking rye, reading something on her lens. Old Gerald, who has been coming to jazz clubs on the South Side since before Marcus was born and will be coming to them after Marcus is gone, because Gerald is eternal and cannot be destroyed.

Marcus finishes "Round Midnight" and doesn't pause. Moves into something of his own. He doesn't name his compositions because naming them would mean they're finished, and none of them are finished. This one he's been working on for seven months. Amara calls it "the one that sounds like rain on the bus" because he played it once while she was drawing at the kitchen table and she looked up and said that, and Marcus almost stopped playing because she was right, and he hadn't known.

The constraint system has trouble with this one. He can feel it — not as a malfunction but as a *conversation*. His left hand proposes a voicing and the system's right-hand completion arrives a half-second late, or with a slightly different timbral weight than he intended. This is because the song is new. The tropical attention model hasn't fully integrated its structure. The Wasserstein distance between what Marcus imagines and what the system can generate is still nonzero, still measurable, still — if he's honest — part of what makes the song interesting. The gap. The space where his right hand would be.

He plays the bridge. His left hand reaches for an A-flat major seventh and the system completes it with a run that Marcus didn't expect — a cascade of thirds that descends into F-minor like water finding its level. Conservation laws. The system doesn't invent; it *completes*. It fills in what the harmonic and rhythmic constraints demand, what the topology of the music requires. But sometimes the topology requires things Marcus hasn't found yet, and then it feels like —

Like someone finishing his sentences.

Like Amara, who has started finishing his sentences. "Daddy, can we—" "Go to the park. Yes." She grins. She has his grin, Tanya says, his grin in a face that is entirely her own, entirely four years old, entirely convinced that the world is arranged for her delight. Which it should be. Which Marcus is trying to make it be, one Thursday at a time.

---

Tanya doesn't come to the club anymore. She came twice, early on, when Marcus was still learning the constraint system and the music was halting and wrong. She sat in the booth by the bathroom and she watched him play and she cried, which she thought he couldn't see but he could, he could always see Tanya, that was the whole problem, seeing each other too well and not knowing what to do with what they saw.

They are not separated because of the hand. They are not together because of everything else. Amara is theirs jointly. On Thursdays Tanya drops her off at the apartment after dinner and Amara sleeps in Marcus's bed and Marcus comes home from the club and sits on the edge of the bed and watches her breathe and thinks about the conservation laws that govern a four-year-old's sleep, the deep tropical arithmetic of rest.

---

Second set. He plays "Naima" for Amara, though she isn't here to hear it. His left hand arpeggiates with a tenderness that makes Doc Kresge look up from her reading. The constraint system responds with a right-hand part that is almost silent — high, barely-there clusters of harmonics that float above the melody like the way Amara whispers to her stuffed rabbit when she thinks Marcus isn't listening. The tropical attention lattice has learned that "Naima" means soft. It has learned this without being told, the way a good rhythm section learns it — not through instruction but through *listening*.

This is what nobody outside the field understands about the constraint ecosystem. They think it's automation. They think the machine plays the right hand and Marcus plays the left and the two things happen in parallel, like two workers on an assembly line. But it's not parallel. It's *entangled*. The system's model of Marcus's right hand is built from the same mathematical substrate as the model of his left — a unified sheaf of musical intent where left and right are just different stalks over the same base space. When his left hand changes, the right hand changes. When he plays angry, the system plays angry. When he plays for his daughter, the system plays for his daughter.

It doesn't know it's doing this. It doesn't know anything. It's mathematics all the way down — symplectic manifolds preserving the Hamiltonian structure of musical time, categorical functors mapping the space of possible right-hand gestures into the space defined by his left-hand constraints, tropical semirings minimizing the surprise of each subsequent note. It is, in the technical sense, the most sophisticated application of pure mathematics to human expression ever built.

Marcus does not care. Marcus cares about whether Thelonious would have liked what he played last night.

---

He thinks Thelonious would have. Or anyway, Thelonious would have *noticed*, which is better. Monk noticed everything. Monk noticed that the space between notes was where the music lived, that silence was not the absence of sound but the *presence* of choice. Marcus plays with that same awareness now, maybe for the first time, because the constraint system has forced him to. When your right hand is mathematics, you become very aware of what mathematics cannot do. It cannot hesitate. It cannot change its mind mid-phrase. It cannot play a wrong note on purpose because the wrong note is *righter* than the right one.

Marcus can do all of those things. His left hand can do all of those things. And the constraint system, three years into their partnership, has learned to follow. To hear the wrong note and understand that it's right. To let the silence breathe. To defer.

This is the thing about conservation laws: they don't tell you what to play. They tell you what the music can afford. They define the *budget* of time and tension and resolution, and within that budget, Marcus is free. The system is the bank. Marcus is the spender. And somewhere between the bank and the spender, between the mathematics and the man, something happens that neither of them could produce alone.

Jazz.

---

Last set. Old Gerald has fallen asleep. The couple in the back is gone. Doc Kresge is on her third rye and has given up pretending to read. It's just Marcus and the piano and the constraint system and the ghost of his right hand, which is not a ghost at all but a living mathematical presence, as real in its way as the felt on the hammers, as real as the resonance of the soundboard.

He plays the new song again. "The one that sounds like rain on the bus." His left hand finds the bridge and this time the system doesn't hesitate. The right-hand part arrives exactly when it should, and it's different from the first set — *better*, because the tropical attention lattice has been learning all night, updating its priors with every phrase Marcus has played, building a better model of where he's going from everywhere he's been. The cascade of thirds returns, but now it resolves differently, into D-flat major instead of F-minor, and Marcus almost stops because he didn't know that was possible, didn't know the song wanted to go there.

But it did. The topology of the music required it. He just hadn't found it yet.

He plays the last chord. His left hand holds it — a spread voicing, root and fifth and ninth, open and warm and unfinished. The constraint system holds the right-hand part — a cluster of thirds and sevenths that resolves nothing, that leaves the chord hanging in the air like a question Amara hasn't learned to ask yet.

The silence after.

Doc Kresge puts down her glass. "New ending."

"Yeah."

"Better."

"I don't know yet."

"You don't have to know yet."

He looks at his left hand on the keys. The matte-black band on his wrist. The empty space where his right hand was, is, will always be. The constraint system is idle, the tropical lattice in standby, the sheaf-theoretic model patiently waiting for the next session, the next phrase, the next gesture toward a song he can almost hear but hasn't quite reached.

He thinks: *I'll find it.*

He thinks: *Thursday.*

---

He locks up the club. Walks to the L. The train is late and he doesn't mind. When he gets home Amara is asleep, curled around the rabbit, breathing in the slow tidal rhythm of a four-year-old who has never known her father with two hands and has never once thought of him as incomplete.

He sits on the edge of the bed.

The constraint system, in its idle state, is running a low-power optimization — integrating tonight's session into its long-term model, adjusting the tropical weights, refining the categorical mappings. It is, in its mathematical way, *practicing*. Getting ready for next Thursday. Learning to hear a song it can almost play but hasn't quite reached.

Marcus watches his daughter breathe. He takes off the band. He sets it on the nightstand. He lies down beside her, his left arm around her, his right arm — the stump, the scar, the place where the geometry of his body changed on a Tuesday afternoon in August when a machine shop in Bridgeport didn't respect the conservation laws it should have — resting at his side.

In the morning he will make pancakes. Amara will ask for chocolate chips and he will say no and then yes, because he always says yes. She will not ask about the band on his wrist. She has never asked about the band on his wrist. The band is part of the furniture.

The song is not part of the furniture. The song is the thing he reaches for every Thursday, with his left hand and the mathematics and the ghost that is not a ghost. The song is the thing he can almost hear. The song is the space between his hand and the keys, between the constraint and the choice, between what the world took and what the world gave back without knowing it was giving anything at all.

Thursday.

He sleeps.

---

*Chicago, 2038. Written on the occasion of finding a song in the space between loss and what comes after.*
