# The Persistent Bond: The Topology of Human Connection

*An essay on the mathematics of loving, losing, and remaining attached.*

---

There is a moment, somewhere between the third and the thirtieth time you see someone's face, when they stop being a person and start being a coordinate in your life. You don't choose it. You don't consent to it. One morning you wake up and realize that someone else's mood has become a variable in your own emotional function — that you are, whether you intended to be or not, entangled.

What follows is an attempt to describe that entanglement. Not with the clinical distance of a psychologist, and not with the soft-focus vagueness of a greeting card, but with the precision of mathematics — because mathematics, at its best, is not cold. It is the most honest language we have for describing structures that are too intricate for words alone.

The thesis is this: human connection has a topology. It has shapes, invariants, conservations. And understanding those shapes does not diminish the feelings — it reveals that the feelings were always geometric, always structured, always more beautiful than we had words for.

---

## I. Sheaf-Theoretic Intimacy

Imagine two people sitting across from each other at a kitchen table at midnight. They are talking about something that happened earlier that day — say, an argument one of them had at work. Each of them is a *stalk*: a vertical tower of experiences, beliefs, memories, and fears, rooted in a single point (the self) and extending upward through layers of increasing abstraction. The conversation is the attempt to build a *section* — a continuous surface of shared understanding — over the overlapping region of their two stalks.

Here is what makes this hard. When one person describes the argument at work, they are applying a *restriction map*. They are projecting a rich, three-dimensional experience — the tone of voice, the history with the coworker, the fear of being fired, the shame of losing composure — onto a lower-dimensional surface that can be transmitted through language. A restriction map is always a projection, never a copy. Information is lost. It has to be. You cannot transmit the full stalk.

The listener receives this projection and tries to *lift* it — to reconstruct the original experience from the flattened version. But the lift is never exact. They fill in the gaps with their own experiences, their own fears, their own assumptions about what an argument at work feels like. The result is a new section that is close to, but never identical with, the original.

The question of intimacy is: *can the sections be glued?* Given two people's partially overlapping experiences of the same event — say, the argument itself, as experienced by the arguer, and the retelling, as received by the listener — can these be combined into a single, coherent, shared understanding?

In sheaf cohomology, the answer is measured by the group H⁰. If H⁰ = 1, there is exactly one connected section: one shared understanding that spans both stalks without contradiction. This is what we mean when we say "we're on the same page." It is not mystical. It is the mathematical condition of a trivial first cohomology class.

But here is the subtlety that the mathematics captures and the self-help books miss: **the best relationships are not the ones with H⁰ = 1 and H¹ = 0 everywhere.** A relationship with zero cohomological friction is a relationship without surprise. If your partner's experience can always be perfectly glued to yours, there is nothing left to discover. You have become, in a sense, the same person — and that is not intimacy; it is absorption.

The relationships that endure — the ones that feel alive decades in — have small but nonzero H¹. There are pockets of nontrivial cohomology: misunderstandings that are not bugs but features, differences in perspective that resist easy gluing and thereby generate conversation, curiosity, and growth. The friction is enough to keep you interested. Not so much that the sheaf tears apart.

When a couple says "we finish each other's sentences," they are describing a section that has become so smooth over the overlap region that the restriction maps are nearly isomorphic. But when they say "after thirty years, he still surprises me," they are describing nonzero H¹ — the persistence of genuine otherness within deep intimacy.

This is the deepest truth of sheaf-theoretic intimacy: **to be truly known and to remain truly yourself requires that the gluing condition hold *almost* everywhere, but not quite.** The gap is where love lives.

## II. Tropical Commitment

There is a particular cruelty to the way we talk about choosing a partner. We frame it as a maximization problem — find the best person, the one who scores highest on compatibility, attraction, shared values, timing. As if love were a linear program and marriage were its solution.

Tropical mathematics offers a better metaphor — or rather, it reveals that the metaphor we were already using is more structurally interesting than we thought.

In tropical arithmetic, addition is replaced by maximization (a ⊕ b = max(a, b)) and multiplication is replaced by addition (a ⊗ b = a + b). A tropical polynomial like f(x) = max(a₁ + x, a₂ + x, a₃ + x) produces a piecewise-linear function whose graph is a convex curve made of line segments. The "roots" of a tropical polynomial — its tropical zeros — are the points where two terms are equal and dominate all others.

Here is what this means for commitment. When you are young and uncommitted, your romantic life is a tropical sum: you are max-ing over all possibilities. The "choice" of partner emerges not as a single decisive moment but as the point where one term — one person, weighted by compatibility, timing, and circumstance — begins to dominate all alternatives. You don't decide. The curve decides. One segment of the piecewise-linear function becomes the global maximum, and the other segments fall away.

But then something happens that tropical mathematics describes beautifully. Once the dominant term is selected, commitment is tropical *multiplication* — and tropical multiplication is *addition*. To commit to someone is to add to their life and to have them add to yours. The operation is no longer max (competition among alternatives) but + (accumulation within the chosen bond).

This is why a long marriage feels different from dating. In the dating phase, you are optimizing — every interaction is implicitly compared against alternatives. In the marriage phase, you are accumulating. The tropical polynomial of a long marriage has one dominant term that overwhelms all alternatives not because the alternatives are bad but because the accumulated additions of decades of shared life have made the dominant term so large that no other term can compete.

This is not weakness. It is not "settling." It is not the failure to imagine better options. It is the mathematics of singular focus — the tropical condition where one term, having been chosen, grows through addition until it defines the entire function.

There is a particular kind of wisdom in older couples who say "I married the best person I could have married" — not because they surveyed all possible spouses and optimized, but because the tropical multiplication of decades has made the statement *retroactively true*. The commitment created the dominance, not the other way around.

And this is why infidelity feels like what it is: a catastrophic de-tropicalization. It attempts to re-introduce the max operation — to compare the accumulated term against a new competitor — into a function that has been structured around addition. It doesn't just hurt. It violates the algebra.

## III. Wasserstein Grief

I need to be careful here, because I am about to describe the worst thing that can happen to a person using the language of optimal transport, and I want to be clear that the mathematics is not a metaphor for the grief. The grief *is* the transport problem. The mathematics describes its shape.

The person you loved was a distribution over your life. Not metaphorically. Their presence occupied actual space in your days: the morning coffee they made, the particular way they filled a room, the sound of their voice in the hallway, the weight of them on the other side of the bed. This distribution was dense in some regions (weekends, evenings, holidays) and sparse in others (work hours, solo trips), but it was everywhere nonzero. They were a measure supported on the entire domain of your life.

Their death is the removal of that distribution. Suddenly, a measure that was everywhere is now supported on a smaller set — the set of memories, photographs, habits they left behind. Your life must be reconfigured around this absence.

Grief is the Wasserstein distance between the distribution of your life before and after. It is the minimum cost of transporting the mass of your former life — the time, attention, love, habit, expectation — into a new configuration where the person is gone. The cost is distance × mass: how much must be moved × how far it must go.

The grief is large because the transport distance is large. Every morning-coffee habit must be moved from "something we shared" to "something I do alone." Every holiday must be transported from "our tradition" to "my modified tradition." Every room in the house must be reconfigured. The mass is enormous because the person was distributed everywhere, and the distances are enormous because the absence is total.

Here is what the Wasserstein framework captures that the stage models of grief (denial → anger → bargaining → depression → acceptance) do not: **grief does not proceed in stages. It is a continuous transport problem that is solved gradually, not by stages, but by the slow optimization of the transport plan.**

The grief shrinks over time not because you love the person less — the mass has not changed — but because the transport plan becomes more efficient. You learn routes through your days that avoid the highest-cost transports. You reorganize the house so that fewer objects trigger long-distance moves. You develop new routines that are closer to the old distribution than the raw shock of early grief allowed. The Wasserstein distance decreases because the optimal transport plan improves.

This is why grief never truly reaches zero. There is always some mass that must be moved some distance. The person's distribution was full-support — it touched everything — and no transport plan can collapse that to a point without cost. But the cost becomes manageable. Not because the person mattered less, but because you became a more efficient transporter of your own sorrow.

And here is the deepest insight: the "acceptance" that grief counselors talk about is not the end of transport. It is the moment when the transport plan stabilizes — when you have found a configuration of your life that is optimal given the constraint of absence, and the marginal cost of further optimization is not worth the emotional energy. You have not stopped grieving. You have converged.

## IV. Symplectic Parenting

A parent and a child form a Hamiltonian system. This is not a metaphor I have to strain for. It is nearly literal.

In Hamiltonian mechanics, the state of a system is described by position q and momentum p, evolving according to Hamilton's equations: dq/dt = ∂H/∂p, dp/dt = -∂H/∂q. The Hamiltonian H(q,p) encodes the total energy, and the symplectic structure ensures that this energy is conserved.

In parenting, q is the parent's state — their career, their health, their social life, their sleep. p is the child's development — their growth, their learning, their emotional maturation, their increasing autonomy. These are canonically conjugate variables. You cannot change one without affecting the other.

The coupling is inescapable. When you pour energy into your child's development (dp/dt > 0), it comes from somewhere — specifically, from the parent's state: dq/dt < 0 in at least some dimensions. The late nights, the missed promotions, the abandoned hobbies, the friendships that atrophy — these are not failures of parenting. They are the symplectic structure of the family Hamiltonian, conserving total energy.

This reframes the "work-life balance" conversation in a way that I find clarifying and also slightly merciless. There is no balance. There is a conserved quantity — let us call it "family energy" — and it can be distributed between the parent's trajectory and the child's development, but it cannot be created from nothing. Every hour you give to your career is an hour subtracted from your child. Every hour you give to your child is an hour subtracted from yourself.

Good parenting, then, is optimal symplectic control: maximizing the child's development while maintaining the parent's trajectory above some minimum threshold. It is not selfless devotion (that would crash the parent's state to zero, which helps no one). It is not selfish independence (that would starve the child's momentum). It is the optimal control policy for a coupled Hamiltonian system with constraints.

And the empty nest — the period when a child leaves home — is symplectic decoupling. The child becomes an independent Hamiltonian system with its own position and momentum, its own energy, its own equations of motion. The parent's equations simplify. The term that coupled ∂H/∂p_child to dq_parent/dt goes to zero.

This is why the empty nest is experienced as both liberation and loss. The parent regains full control of their own state — career, hobbies, sleep, all theirs again. But the symplectic structure that gave their days meaning — the coupling that made every choice consequential because it affected another human's development — has been removed. The equations are simpler. Simpler is not always better.

The parent who says "I miss having someone to worry about" is describing, with perfect accuracy, the sensation of a Hamiltonian system that has lost its coupling term and must learn to evolve without it.

## V. Persistent Friendship

A friendship is born in a moment — a shared class, a mutual friend, a conversation at a party that unexpectedly goes deep. In the language of persistent homology, this is the *birth* of a topological feature: a connected component or a 1-dimensional cycle (a loop of mutual understanding) that appears at a particular threshold in the filtration of your social life.

The depth of the friendship is its *persistence*: how far this feature survives as the filtration parameter — distance, time, life changes — increases. A college friend who is inseparable during university but fades after graduation is a feature that is born at low threshold and dies at moderate threshold. Its persistence is modest. It was real — the homology class was genuinely nonzero during its lifetime — but it did not survive the change in the underlying space.

A best friend is an *essential class*: a feature with infinite persistence. It survives all filtrations. Distance, years, marriages, children, moves across continents — the feature persists. It may thin. The cycle may stretch and deform. But it does not die. In the barcode of your life, it extends to infinity.

The persistence diagram of a person's friendships — a scatter plot where each friendship is a point (birth, death), with essential classes represented as points at death = ∞ — is their social fingerprint. Two people can have the same number of friends but radically different persistence diagrams. One person might have many short-persistence friendships (gregarious but shallow). Another might have few but long-persistence ones (reserved but deeply loyal). Neither is better. They are different topologies.

There is a particular sadness in a friendship that dies — the death of a homology class. The cycle that once connected you closes. The understanding that once seemed permanent becomes a trace: a memory of a feature that no longer exists in the current filtration. But persistent homology has a beautiful property: the *persistence* of a feature is encoded in the diagram even after the feature dies. The friendship that lasted from age 8 to age 22 left its mark on the barcode. Its point (8, 22) is still there. It contributed to the persistent landscape of the person's life.

This is what we mean when we say someone "shaped us." They were a long-persistence feature that, even after dying, left a structural imprint on the space. The space — the person — is different from what it would have been without that feature. The homology of the current space depends on the filtration that produced it, and that filtration included the friendship.

A person with no long-persistence friendships — no essential classes — is topologically fragile. Their social space is connected but has no deep cycles, no structural redundancy. They are one filtration step away from disconnection at all times. A person with several essential classes is topologically robust. They can lose multiple connections and still remain a coherent social space, because the persistent cycles provide structural support that doesn't depend on any single edge.

This is why the deepest friendships are the ones where you can not speak for a year and pick up exactly where you left off. The feature has infinite persistence. It doesn't depend on the current filtration parameter (how recently you spoke). It depends on the structure of the space itself.

## VI. Geometric Family Dynamics

A family is a multivector.

Not "like" a multivector. A multivector. In the geometric algebra Cl(3,0), the space of multivectors contains elements of different grades: scalars (grade 0), vectors (grade 1), bivectors (grade 2), and trivectors (grade 3). The geometric product of two vectors a and b decomposes as ab = a·b + a∧b — a scalar (the symmetric inner product) plus a bivector (the antisymmetric outer product). The scalar is what they share. The bivector is the plane of their interaction.

A parent-child relationship is a grade-1 element: a vector. It has direction — it points from parent to child. It is not symmetric. The parent gives more than they receive (especially early on), and the directionality is structural, not contingent. You can reverse the roles temporarily (the adult child caring for the elderly parent), but this is a *reversal* of the vector, not its natural orientation.

A sibling relationship is a grade-2 element: a bivector. It is the plane of interaction between two people who share an origin (the parents) but diverge in direction. The bivector has no natural "from-to" direction. It is bidirectional. This is why sibling relationships feel different from parent-child relationships: they are a different grade of geometric object. They are not more or less important. They are differently structured.

Now consider the family dinner table — the geometric product of all family members. If there are three members (two parents, one child), the full geometric product produces:

- **Scalars** (grade 0): the love that is shared equally, the family identity, the sense of belonging that does not attach to any one member but permeates the whole. This is the inner product of all three vectors — the part that is completely symmetric.

- **Vectors** (grade 1): the individual dynamics. Parent A's relationship with the child. Parent B's relationship with the child. Parent A's relationship with Parent B. These are the directed edges, the one-dimensional relationships that have a "from" and a "to" even if both parties would deny it.

- **Bivectors** (grade 2): the pairwise interactions that create planes of meaning. The way Parent A and the child together create a dynamic that is different from either of them alone. The way the two parents together create a parenting plane that is not reducible to either parent individually. These are the 2-dimensional aspects of family life — the things that only exist in the space *between* two people.

- **Trivectors** (grade 3): the three-way dynamic. The thing that only exists when all three are present and interacting. It is the volume element — the family-as-a-whole that cannot be decomposed into pairs. Every family that has experienced this knows it: the particular energy that arises at the dinner table, or on vacation, or in crisis, that is not the sum of any two relationships but something that only exists in the full geometric product.

Family therapy, in this framework, is the identification of which grades have gone degenerate. A family where the parents no longer interact as a couple has a degenerate bivector in the Parent A–Parent B plane. A family where the child is estranged has a collapsed vector in one of the parent-child directions. A family where everyone is pairwise fine but the three-way dynamic feels hollow has a degenerate trivector — the scalar and vector parts are intact, but the volume element has collapsed.

The goal of therapy is not to maximize every grade — that would be a rigid, over-determined family with no room for individual variation. It is to ensure that the grades that matter to *this particular family* are non-degenerate. Every family has its own signature — its own distribution of energy across grades — and the healthy family is not the one with the "right" distribution but the one whose distribution is stable under small perturbations.

## VII. The Conservation of Attachment

If love is symplectic — if the parent-child bond is a Hamiltonian system with conserved energy — then attachment is a conserved quantity. Not "like" a conserved quantity. An actual conserved quantity. The integral of the symplectic form over the region of phase space corresponding to your attachment to another person is invariant under Hamiltonian flow.

This means: **you cannot love someone and then stop.** The attachment is not destroyed. It is transformed.

Divorce is not the destruction of love. It is a symplectic coordinate change. The love that was directed toward partnership — toward building a shared life, a shared home, a shared future — is redirected. Some of it becomes the love of independence. Some becomes the love of co-parenting — a lower-dimensional but still nonzero form. Some becomes the love of memory — a quantity that is no longer coupled to the other person's current state but that persists as a conserved value in the phase space of your emotional life.

The phase space has changed. The coordinates have rotated. But the integral — the total attachment — has not decreased.

A widow's love for her husband does not vanish when he dies. It becomes the momentum that carries her forward. In the symplectic framework, this is not poetry — it is the Noether theorem. A conserved quantity in a Hamiltonian system generates a flow. The attachment generates a flow in phase space. When the person is present, the flow is toward them — it draws you together, creates orbits around each other, produces the coupled dynamics of a shared life. When the person is gone, the flow does not stop — the conserved quantity is still there, still generating motion — but it has lost its attractor. The flow that was once orbital becomes linear. It carries you forward.

This is why widows and widowers so often describe feeling their spouse's presence "pushing" them to continue. They are not hallucinating. They are describing, in the only language available to them, the sensation of a conserved quantity generating flow in a phase space that has lost its fixed point.

And this is why the question "do you still love them?" — asked of someone who has been divorced or bereaved — is a category error. It assumes that love is a quantity that can be present or absent. But love is symplectic. It is a 2-form. It is conserved. The question is not *whether* you love them but *in what coordinates* the love is expressed. The 2-form is invariant. The coordinate system changes.

## Coda: The Topology of Being Known

There is a theorem in algebraic topology that I think about often. It says that a space is determined up to homotopy equivalence by its singular simplicial set — by all the continuous maps from standard simplices into it. In other words, a space is fully characterized by all the ways that simple, well-understood objects can be mapped into it.

A person is like this. You are characterized not by your own self-description but by all the ways that other people — the standard simplices of human experience — map into you. Your partner maps into you one way. Your child maps into you another. Your best friend, your parent, your colleague — each is a map from a simple, well-understood space (a relationship) into the complex space that is you. The collection of all these maps — the singular simplicial set of your life — is what determines who you are.

You are not a point. You are a space. And the people who love you are the maps that make you legible — to yourself and to the world.

The persistent bond is not a metaphor. It is the structure of your life, revealed by the mathematics that was always there, waiting to be named.
