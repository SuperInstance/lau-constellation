# The Boundary Conditions

Adaeze was seventeen when she built the thing that should have saved them, and eighteen when she learned that saving and helping were different countries with no road between them.

Her workshop was a folding table outside her mother's groundnut stall in Mushin, wedged between the gutter and a pyramidal stack of Fanta crates. The laptop was secondhand, cracked across the Dell logo so it read D LL, and it connected to the mesh through a relay someone had zip-tied to a telephone pole three streets over. The connection was erratic. Lagos was many things, but stable was not among them.

She had found the SuperInstance libraries the way her generation found everything — through the open repositories, through the forums where people who understood mathematics spoke to people who didn't, through the slow miracle of knowledge that wanted to be free. Tropical attention mechanisms. She had liked the sound of it before she understood it. *Tropical.* As though mathematics had a climate.

It did, in a way. The tropical semiring replaced addition with maximum and multiplication with addition, which sounded like a parlor trick until you watched it carve decision boundaries through visual data like a river cutting through laterite. The phone camera captured light. The tropical geometry found the shapes within the light that meant something was wrong. Skin lesions. The yellowing of sclerae. The clubbing of nail beds. The library did the heavy lifting; Adaeze had merely — and this word would later make her furious when investors used it, because *merely* was a word people used to describe work they couldn't do — adapted it to the diagnostic patterns Dr. Okafor had listed in his 2021 public-health paper on low-resource dermatological screening.

She called it Ilera. Health, in Yoruba. She was Yoruba the way Lagos was Yoruba, which was to say completely and also not at all, because Lagos belonged to everyone who could survive it.

---

Mama Ade ran the clinic on Adeniyi Jones Avenue the way she had since before Adaeze's mother was born. It was not a clinic in the way the universities meant the word. It was two rooms behind a green door with the paint flaking off in continents, a cot, a cabinet of supplies she purchased herself from the pharmacy on the corner, and thirty years of knowing what sick looked like.

She was sixty-three. She had the hands of someone who had touched ten thousand foreheads to check for fever, and the silence of someone who had sat with people who were dying in rooms too small for dying to happen gracefully. She had delivered babies who were now mothers themselves. She had bandaged wounds that the young men didn't want to talk about. She knew the difference between a rash that would pass and a rash that meant the hospital, and she could not have told you how she knew, which was precisely the problem, because the new mathematics demanded that knowledge explain itself.

Adaeze had come to her on a Tuesday in March, during the quiet hour between the morning rush and the afternoon heat.

"I've built something," she said.

Mama Ade was sorting gauze. She did not look up. "You and every young person with a telephone."

"It sees sickness. Through the camera. It can tell you what's wrong with someone's skin, or their eyes, or—"

"I can see sickness with my own eyes, Adaeze. I have been doing it since before your mother learned to walk."

"I know." And this was true. Adaeze respected Mama Ade the way you respect weather — completely, without negotiation. You could not argue with rain and you could not argue with Mama Ade's thirty years, and Adaeze did not want to. What she wanted was to give those thirty years a tool.

"The machine does not replace you," she said carefully. "It is like a second opinion. You look at someone and you feel something is wrong. This can help you name it."

Mama Ade tied off the gauze and placed it in the cabinet with the care of a woman organizing her own skeleton. "A machine does not feel," she said.

"No," Adaeze agreed. "It calculates."

"And calculating is better than feeling?"

It was a question that had been asked in different forms in different centuries, and the answer was always the same and always unsatisfying: it depended. A machine could spot the early margin of a melanoma that a human eye would dismiss as a mole. A human could look at a patient and know, with perfect and inexplicable certainty, that this person was not going to recover, and adjust everything — tone, posture, the words chosen — accordingly. The machine could find the boundary. The human could understand what the boundary meant.

"I'm not asking you to trust it," Adaeze said. "I'm asking you to try it."

Mama Ade looked at her. She had the gaze of someone who had been disappointed by promises many times and had learned to absorb the disappointment without flinching, which was worse than flinching, because it meant she had stopped expecting anything else.

"Bring it," she said. "We will see."

---

She brought it the next day. The phone was her own, a Tecno with a cracked screen protector and a battery that held charge like a sieve held water. Ilera ran on it because she had designed it to run on phones like hers — this was the point, this was the whole point, that the people who needed it most had the least, and a diagnostic tool that required a flagship device was a diagnostic tool for people who already had doctors.

The first patient was a woman with a rash on her forearm. Mama Ade looked at it and said, "Eczema. She should stop using that soap."

Adaeze held the phone over the woman's arm. Ilera processed. The tropical geometry unfolded in the phone's modest processor — min-plus algebra finding the skeleton of the visual data, attention mechanisms weighting the features that mattered — and returned a result.

"Contact dermatitis," Adaeze read. "Ninety-two percent confidence. Possible allergic reaction to—"

"The soap," Mama Ade said. "I just told you."

But she was looking at the phone with an expression that was not quite curiosity and not quite hostility. It was the expression of someone watching a dog walk on its hind legs: not that it was done well, but that it was done at all.

The second patient was a boy of eight with a fever and red eyes. Mama Ade checked his temperature, looked at his throat, pressed the glands beneath his jaw.

"Malaria," she said.

The phone agreed: *Conjunctival findings consistent with febrile illness. Scleral pattern suggests malaria — 87% confidence. Recommend rapid test.*

"The test I was going to give him anyway," Mama Ade said.

The third patient was an old man with a growth on his neck. Mama Ade looked at it for a long time. She touched it with her fingers. She asked him questions — how long, does it hurt, any other places.

"I don't know," she said finally. "This one needs the hospital."

Adaeze held up the phone. Ilera rendered its judgment in four seconds: *Squamous cell carcinoma — 78% confidence. Basal cell carcinoma — 14%. Recommend biopsy.*

The word *carcinoma* arrived like a stone dropped into still water. The old man looked at Adaeze. He looked at the phone. He looked at Mama Ade.

"It's cancer?" he said.

"We don't know yet," Mama Ade said, and her voice carried thirty years of saying things people didn't want to hear in rooms too small for the hearing of them. "The machine thinks it might be. I think you need to see a doctor who can cut a piece and look at it under a glass. That is all we know."

She turned to Adaeze after the man left. "Seventy-eight percent."

"Yes."

"So there is a twenty-two percent chance it is wrong."

"Yes."

"And you want me to send people to the hospital — to spend money they do not have, to take a day they cannot afford — on something that is wrong almost one time in four."

It was a fair question. It was the fair question. Adaeze had asked it of herself many times, sitting at the folding table with the D LL laptop, watching the loss functions converge, watching the accuracy climb.

"The alternative is that you don't know either," she said. "You said so. You didn't know."

"I didn't know," Mama Ade agreed. "And so I would have told him to watch it. Come back in two weeks. See if it changes. That is not the same as telling a man he has cancer when there is a one-in-five chance he does not."

"He doesn't have to go today. He can watch it too. But now he knows to watch it harder."

"Does he? Or does he go home and sit with the word *cancer* in his mouth like a stone he cannot swallow? Does he sleep tonight? Does he tell his wife?"

Adaeze did not answer, because there was no answer that was true for everyone. Mama Ade was right. The machine was also right. These things could both be true, and the space between them was where people actually lived.

---

The weeks that followed were a lesson in the distance between correct and useful.

Adaeze brought the phone every day. Mama Ade let her use it, grudgingly at first and then with something that was not quite acceptance but was no longer refusal. They developed a rhythm: Mama Ade examined the patient first, gave her assessment, and then Adaeze held up the phone. Sometimes they agreed. Sometimes they didn't. When they didn't, something interesting happened.

A young woman came in with fatigue and yellowing eyes. Mama Ade said hepatitis. Ilera said malaria, with a secondary flag for possible anemia. The rapid test came back positive for malaria. Mama Ade nodded, unsurprised, and treated her.

But two weeks later the woman returned, worse, and the hospital — where Mama Ade had by then sent her — confirmed hepatitis B.

"Your machine was wrong," Mama Ade said.

"Your eyes were right," Adaeze said.

"Yes. And if we had listened to the machine, we would have treated her for malaria and sent her home to get sicker. What do you call a tool that makes you trust it and then kills you?"

"A bad tool," Adaeze said.

"A dangerous tool," Mama Ade corrected. "Bad is a hammer with a loose head. Dangerous is a thing that makes you stop using your own eyes."

---

That night, at the folding table, Adaeze opened the code and stared at it. The tropical attention weights were sound. The decision boundaries were clean. The model had been trained on forty thousand images and validated on ten thousand more. It had been right about the yellowing — it had seen the scleral patterns. But it had weighted the malaria features higher because in this region, in this population, malaria was more common, and the priors reflected that. The mathematics was doing what mathematics did: optimizing for the most probable answer given the data it had.

Mama Ade had done something different. She had looked at the woman's eyes and felt something — not a calculation, not a probability, but the accumulation of thirty years of watching yellow eyes mean different things in different contexts. She had seen hepatitis before, in eyes that looked like this, in a woman who moved like this, in a season when malaria was common and hepatitis was unexpected and therefore easy to dismiss.

She had been wrong about the malaria too, in the initial assessment. But she had been right about the hepatitis, and she had been right to send the woman to the hospital, and the phone had not told her to do that. The phone had said malaria with high confidence and the matter was closed, as far as the mathematics was concerned.

Adaeze spent that night and the next three rebuilding the confidence calibrator. When Ilera was uncertain — when the tropical boundary was thin, when the attention spread across multiple diagnoses with comparable weights — it should say so. Not a number. Not *78% confidence.* But words: *This could be several things. Look harder.*

On the fourth day she showed it to Mama Ade.

"I made it dumber," she said.

Mama Ade looked at the screen, where the interface now displayed a simple message: **NOT SURE. COULD BE MALARIA OR HEPATITIS. CHECK BOTH.**

"Good," Mama Ade said. And for the first time, she reached for the phone herself, without being asked.

---

By July, Ilera was running in eleven clinics across Mushin, Surulere, and Agege. Adaeze had registered it as a nonprofit, because the people who needed it couldn't pay and the people who could pay didn't need it, and she had not built it for them. The SuperInstance libraries made the computation possible on cheap hardware; the open-source community had helped her optimize for the specific patterns of skin disease in equatorial populations, which the original training sets — drawn from American and European hospitals — had underrepresented.

Mama Ade used the phone every day now. She did not trust it. She used it. These were not the same thing. She held it up to rashes and growths and the pale undersides of eyelids, and she read what it said, and then she made her own decision. Sometimes the phone confirmed what she already knew, and she found this gratifying in a way she would never admit. Sometimes it suggested something she hadn't considered, and she would examine the patient again with new eyes. Sometimes it was wrong, and she ignored it, and was right to.

Once, in August, it caught something she had missed. A three-year-old girl with a spot on her arm that Mama Ade had dismissed as a bruise. Ilera flagged it as a possible indicator of a bleeding disorder, low confidence but worth investigating. Mama Ade had been irritated. She had sent the child's mother to the hospital anyway, because a machine telling you to be careful was annoying in the same way a smoke alarm was annoying: loud, graceless, and occasionally the reason you were still alive.

The hospital confirmed a mild form of von Willebrand disease. The child would be fine, now that they knew.

Mama Ade did not thank the phone. She did not thank Adaeze. She sat in her clinic after closing and stared at the wall for a long time, and then she got up and reorganized her supply cabinet, which was what she did when she was processing something she couldn't yet name.

---

Adaeze understood something that year, sitting between the folding table and the green door, between the mathematics that could find the shape of disease in light and the woman who could find the shape of a person in a glance. The tool was not the point. The tool was never the point. The point was the space between the tool and the hand that held it — the space where judgment lived, where a number became a decision, where ninety-two percent confidence became a sentence spoken to a frightened woman in a language she could bear to hear.

Ilera could see sickness. Mama Ade could see people. The gap between those two things was where medicine actually happened, and it was wider than any boundary the tropical geometry could draw, and it could not be optimized away, because it was not a problem. It was the thing that made the solution possible.

By the end of the year, Adaeze had rewritten the documentation. The first line, which had previously been *Ilera: Affordable AI Diagnostics for Low-Resource Settings*, now read:

*Ilera is a tool. It is not a doctor. Use it to look again. Then decide for yourself.*

Mama Ade, when she saw it, said nothing. But she nodded, once, and the nod contained everything.

---

*Afterword: In 2035, the WHO listed Ilera as a recommended adjunctive screening tool for community health workers in sub-Saharan Africa. The paper cited its "unusually conservative confidence calibration" as a key strength. Adaeze, who was nineteen and presenting at her first international conference, said only: "I learned it from a nurse in Mushin who has been practicing medicine longer than I have been alive. She taught me that the most important thing a tool can do is admit when it doesn't know." The audience applauded. Mama Ade, watching the livestream on a cracked Tecno phone in her clinic in Lagos, turned it off halfway through and went back to work. There were patients waiting.*
