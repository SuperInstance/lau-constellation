# Ars Subtilior & Counter-Evidence to the "ET Caused Rhythmic Complexity" Thesis

**Research Date:** 2026-05-24  
**Purpose:** Honest assessment of evidence that contradicts, complicates, or refines the hypothesis that equal temperament caused Western rhythmic complexity.

---

## 1. Ars Subtilior — The Pre-ET Rhythmic Explosion (c. 1375–1410)

### The Core Fact

The Ars Subtilior is the single strongest counter-example to the "ET caused rhythmic complexity" thesis. This was a school of mostly French composers, centered on Avignon and southern France, who produced music of **extraordinary rhythmic complexity** — the likes of which would not be seen again until the 20th century — **centuries before equal temperament existed.**

As Richard Hoppin wrote: *"Not until the twentieth century did music again reach the most subtle refinements and rhythmic complexities of the manneristic style."*

### Key Composers

| Composer | Known For |
|---|---|
| **Philippus de Caserta** (aka Philipoctus de Caserta) | Multiple works in the Chantilly Codex; the term "ars subtilior" derives from his treatise references |
| **Jacob Senleches** | *La Harpe de melodie* (notated in the shape of a harp); *Fuions de ci*; extreme rhythmic innovation |
| **Matteo da Perugia** | Associated with the Milan cathedral; one of the most prolific Ars Subtilior composers |
| **Baude Cordier** | *Belle, bonne, sage* (heart-shaped notation); *Tout par compas* (circular staff, perpetual canon) |
| **Solage** | *Fumeux fume par fumée* — notorious for chromaticism and extreme range |
| **Antonio Zacara da Teramo** | *Sumite karissime*; one of the most radical notational experimenters |
| **Johannes Ciconia** | Bridge figure between Ars Subtilior and early Renaissance |
| **Trebor** (retrograde of "Robert") | Multiple ballades in the Chantilly Codex |
| **Anthonello de Caserta** | Significant contributor to the style |

### Specific Rhythmic Techniques

The Ars Subtilior composers developed an arsenal of rhythmic techniques that anticipate modern complexity by centuries:

1. **Mensuration canons** — A single notated line read simultaneously at different speeds by different voices (the same technique Ockeghem would later systematize in the *Missa Prolationum*)
2. **Prolation canons** — Voices sing the same music but in different mensuration signs (e.g., one in perfect time, another in imperfect time, creating simultaneous duple and triple meter)
3. **Coloration** — Red or void notation that changes the mensural value of notes (a visual/temporal code that altered rhythmic relationships mid-phrase)
4. **Proportional notation** — Ratios like 3:2:1, 4:3, and more extreme proportions applied simultaneously across voices
5. **Syncopation and displacement** — Systematic shifting of accents against the tactus, creating rhythmic dissonance
6. **Diminution and augmentation** — The same melodic material simultaneously at half, double, or other proportional speeds
7. **Eye music** — Notation shaped into visual forms (hearts, harps, circles) that reflected the musical content — a form of multimedia art
8. **Chromaticism** — *Fumeux fume par fumée* by Solage explores extreme chromatic motion unusual for the 14th century

### The Source Manuscripts

- **Chantilly Codex** (Musée Condé MS 564): The primary source, containing 112 polyphonic works, mostly secular. Dated c. 1350–1400. Includes works by all major Ars Subtilior composers.
- **Modena Codex** (Biblioteca Estense, α.M.5.24): Important secondary source with overlapping and unique repertoire.
- **Turin Manuscript** (Torino J.II.9): Contains the French-Cypriot repertory, showing the style spread as far as Cyprus.

### Why Did This Complexity Arise?

Several intersecting causes:

1. **The Avignon Papal Schism (1378–1417)** — The Western Church had rival popes in Rome and Avignon. Avignon became a rival cultural center with a papal court that needed to demonstrate magnificence. Multiple competing courts (Avignon, Rome, various noble courts) created an environment where compositional virtuosity was a form of **cultural competition**.

2. **Courtly one-upmanship** — Complexity became a marker of sophistication. Composers served aristocratic patrons who valued the arcane and erudite. Musical difficulty = cultural capital.

3. **Notational revolution** — The Ars Nova (c. 1310–1370) had already introduced a vastly more flexible rhythmic notation system (via Philippe de Vitry and Marchetto da Padova). The Ars Subtilior was the **logical extreme** of this notational freedom — composers pushed the system to its limits simply because they could.

4. **Intellectual milieu** — The late 14th century was a period of intense scholastic and mathematical inquiry. Composers were often also theorists, and the exploration of proportional relationships had both musical and philosophical appeal.

### Why Did It End?

The Ars Subtilior collapsed around 1410–1420. Several factors:

1. **The Schism resolved** — The Council of Constance (1414–1418) ended the papal schism, removing the competitive Avignon court ecosystem.
2. **Aesthetic reaction** — The Renaissance aesthetic valued clarity, balanced proportion, and text intelligibility — the antithesis of Ars Subtilior excess. The new humanist values explicitly rejected scholastic complexity.
3. **The style had exhausted itself** — It was a self-limiting explosion: music so complex that only specialists could perform it, and only connoisseurs could appreciate it. It became an evolutionary dead end.
4. **The Dunstable/Dufay revolution** — English composers (especially John Dunstable) introduced a simpler, more consonant style ("la contenance angloise") that continental composers adopted with enthusiasm.

**Key takeaway:** The Ars Subtilior proves that rhythmic complexity can arise from **social competition** (courtly one-upmanship) and **notational innovation** alone, without any change in tuning system. It was Pythagorean tuning throughout.

## 2. Ockeghem and the Franco-Flemish School (1450–1550)

### The Missa Prolationum (c. 1470)

Johannes Ockeghem's *Missa Prolationum* is one of the most extraordinary compositional achievements in Western music. It consists **entirely of mensuration canons** — in each movement, all four voices sing the same musical line, but each voice reads it at a different speed determined by its mensuration sign.

This is functionally identical to Conlon Nancarrow's player piano studies (which superimpose voices at different tempi in complex ratios like e:π), except Ockeghem did it **c. 1470**, roughly **480 years before Nancarrow**.

### How It Works

- Each section of the mass explores a different combination of prolations (time signatures in modern terms)
- Voices enter at different intervals and at different speeds
- The listener hears a single notated line generate four-part counterpoint through pure temporal manipulation
- The technique is analogous to placing a single image through four different lenses simultaneously

### What Tuning Did Ockeghem Use?

Ockeghem worked in **Pythagorean tuning** (and possibly early meantone, which was beginning to appear in the late 15th century). Equal temperament did not exist as a practical tuning system during his lifetime. The 12-TET system would not become standard until roughly the 18th century.

**This is devastating to a strong version of the ET thesis.** If Ockeghem could achieve rhythmic complexity of the highest order — prolation canons that rival 20th-century innovation — in Pythagorean or early meantone tuning, then equal temperament cannot be a *necessary condition* for rhythmic complexity.

### The Broader Franco-Flemish Tradition

Ockeghem wasn't alone:
- **Josquin des Prez** wrote prolation canons (e.g., the Agnus Dei from *Missa L'homme armé super voces musicales*)
- **Pierre de la Rue** composed mensuration canons in the early 16th century
- **Jacob Obrecht** explored complex cantus firmus manipulation
- Ockeghem's *Missa cuiusvis toni* could be sung in **any of the modes** — a radical exploration of modal flexibility

This was a tradition that valued compositional virtuosity as an end in itself, operating entirely within pre-ET tuning systems.

## 3. The Alternative Thesis: Complexity Cycles

### The Pattern

Looking at Western music history, a compelling pattern emerges of **oscillation between complexity and simplicity**:

| Period | Era | Complexity Level |
|---|---|---|
| c. 1300 | Ars Nova | **High** (isorhythm, hocket) |
| c. 1375–1410 | Ars Subtilior | **Very High** (peak of medieval complexity) |
| c. 1420–1500 | Early Renaissance | **Low** (clarity, balanced proportions) |
| c. 1500–1550 | High Renaissance (Josquin generation) | **Medium-High** (canonic virtuosity returns) |
| c. 1550–1600 | Late Renaissance (Palestrina) | **Medium** (counterpoint regulated, codified) |
| c. 1600–1750 | Baroque | **High** (Bach's canons, French overture rhythms, ground bass complexity) |
| c. 1750–1820 | Classical | **Low** (homophonic clarity, regular phrase structure) |
| c. 1820–1900 | Romantic | **High** (Wagner, Strauss, rhythmic disintegration) |
| c. 1900–1950 | Modernist | **Very High** (Stravinsky, Nancarrow, Boulez — 20th-century peak) |
| c. 1950–present | Postmodern | **Variable** (minimalism = simplicity; New Complexity = maximum) |

This looks much more like a **wave function** than a linear progression driven by tuning.

### What Drives the Cycles?

If rhythmic complexity is cyclical, the drivers appear to be:

1. **Social competition** — Courts, institutions, and composers compete through virtuosity (Ars Subtilior courts; 20th-century avant-garde institutions)
2. **Notational/technological affordances** — New tools enable new complexity (Ars Nova notation; player piano; computer notation)
3. **Aesthetic reaction** — Every peak of complexity provokes a simplicity backlash (Renaissance after Ars Subtilior; Classical after Baroque; Minimalism after Serialism)
4. **Individual genius** — Some composers simply push further than their contemporaries (Machaut → Senleches; Ockeghem; Bach; Nancarrow)
5. **Institutional support** — Complexity flourishes when institutions fund specialists (papal courts → Ars Subtilior; universities and foundations → postwar avant-garde)

**Crucially, none of these factors require equal temperament.**

### The Honest Assessment

The cycle thesis has explanatory power that the ET thesis lacks. It explains:
- Why rhythmic complexity existed before ET (Ars Subtilior, Ockeghem)
- Why simplicity returns even after ET exists (Classical era, Minimalism)
- Why non-Western traditions also show complexity oscillations

But the cycle thesis is also somewhat tautological — "complexity goes up and down because it goes up and down" — without a clear causal mechanism. It describes a pattern without fully explaining it.

## 4. The Refinement: ET Didn't START Rhythmic Complexity, It REMOVED the Ceiling

This is the most intellectually honest synthesis of the evidence.

### The Argument

1. **Pre-ET rhythmic complexity was POSSIBLE but OPTIONAL.** The Ars Subtilior and Ockeghem prove that composers could achieve extreme rhythmic complexity without ET. But these were *choices* — eccentric peaks driven by specific social conditions (courtly competition, notational experimentation). When those conditions changed, the complexity vanished.

2. **Post-ET rhythmic complexity became NECESSARY for certain expressive purposes.** Once ET flattened the vertical dimension (eliminating the distinct characters of different keys, reducing the expressive contribution of harmony and intonation), composers had to find other dimensions for expressive differentiation. Rhythm was the most available alternative.

3. **The key distinction: reversibility.**
   - Ars Subtilior complexity was **reversible** — it appeared, peaked, and disappeared when social conditions changed
   - Post-ET complexity appears to be **irreversible** — the 20th century's rhythmic innovations (Stravinsky's polyrhythm, Nancarrow's tempo canons, Ligeti's micropolyphony) did not retreat. Even the "simplicity" of Minimalism (Reich, Glass) is deeply rhythmically complex in ways that would be unrecognizable to the Renaissance.

4. **ET didn't invent horizontal complexity; it made it structurally necessary.** Before ET, harmony carried enormous expressive weight through key-character, pure intervals, and tuning specificity. After ET, those resources were diminished, and rhythm had to compensate. The "rhythmic ceiling" was removed not because ET made rhythm more possible, but because ET made rhythm more *necessary*.

### Testable Implications

If this refined thesis is correct:
- Pre-ET rhythmic complexity should be **localized and reversible** (confirmed: Ars Subtilior)
- Post-ET rhythmic complexity should be **persistent and cumulative** (confirmed: 20th century onward)
- Cultures that never adopted ET should show rhythmic complexity that is **present but non-cumulative** — present when social conditions favor it, but not showing the relentless escalation seen in the post-ET West

### Weaknesses of This Refinement

- It's harder to prove "necessity" than "possibility" — the causal chain from "flattened harmony" to "compensatory rhythm" is inferential
- The 20th-century rhythmic explosion coincides with many other factors (recording technology, world wars, African-American musical influence, colonial encounter with non-Western rhythm) — attributing it primarily to ET is reductive
- The "irreversibility" claim may simply reflect the recency of the 20th-century peak — we may not have enough distance to judge

## 5. Comparative Evidence from Non-Western Traditions

### Indian Classical Music

**Tuning:** Indian classical music (both Hindustani and Carnatic) uses **just intonation-based** shruti system — 22 microtonal divisions of the octave, NOT equal temperament. The raga system is fundamentally non-ET.

**Rhythmic complexity:** This is the **most important comparison** for the thesis.

Indian classical music has an extraordinarily sophisticated rhythmic system — the **tala** — that is arguably more developed than Western rhythmic theory in several dimensions:

- **Cyclical structures:** Talas range from 3 beats to 128 beats per cycle, with the most common being 7–16 beats
- **Hierarchical subdivision:** Each tala has embedded sub-cycles (vibhagas), creating multi-level rhythmic organization
- **Cross-rhythmic improvisation:** Musicians systematically displace and contradict the tala pattern, exploring rhythmic tension and resolution at multiple levels simultaneously
- **No strong/weak beat binary:** Unlike Western meter, talas don't rely on a simple strong-weak hierarchy — accent is determined by musical phrase shape
- **Extremely long cycles:** Some talas have cycles lasting 45+ seconds, requiring performers to maintain large-scale temporal architecture

**Verdict on the ET thesis:** Indian classical music achieves extraordinary rhythmic sophistication **without equal temperament.** The tala system shows that rhythmic complexity of the highest order can develop entirely independently of tuning system changes.

**However:** There is a crucial distinction. Indian rhythmic complexity is primarily **improvisational and cyclical** — it operates within the tala framework as creative play. Western post-ET rhythmic complexity (Nancarrow, Ligeti, Boulez) is primarily **compositional and cumulative** — it builds permanent notated structures of increasing complexity. Whether this difference is due to ET, notation culture, or other factors remains debatable.

### Arabic Maqam Music

**Tuning:** The Arabic maqam system uses microtonal tuning based on 24-tone divisions (in notation), though in practice the intonation is more nuanced and learned orally. It is **emphatically not equal temperament.**

**Rhythmic complexity:** Arabic music has a sophisticated rhythmic component through the **iqa'at** (rhythmic cycles), but there is a critical distinction from the maqam system itself:

- The maqam system defines **melodic** modes and is "subjected to no definite organization" in its rhythmic-temporal component (Touma, 1996)
- Rhythmic cycles (iqa'at) exist as a separate framework, typically played on percussion instruments
- While Arabic rhythm is complex (with cycles like 10/8, 7/8, and additive patterns), the rhythmic innovation is **not primarily driven by the tuning system** — it exists in a different conceptual domain

**Verdict:** Arabic music shows moderate rhythmic complexity coexisting with non-ET tuning, but the relationship appears orthogonal rather than compensatory — rhythm and melody are handled as separate systems rather than being linked through the tuning-complexity dynamic.

### Sub-Saharan African Traditions

Though not requested in the original brief, this is the elephant in the room for any discussion of rhythmic complexity:

- Sub-Saharan African musical traditions (particularly West African drumming, Yoruba, Ewe, Akan) possess rhythmic sophistication that rivals or exceeds any Western tradition
- Polyrhythm, polyrhythmic complexity, cross-rhythm, and metric ambiguity are foundational
- These traditions operate in **non-ET tuning environments** (or in some cases are purely percussive, where tuning is irrelevant)
- This rhythmic sophistication is ancient and pre-colonial

**Verdict:** If the question is "can rhythmic complexity exist without ET?", the answer from African music is an unequivocal **yes.** The most rhythmically sophisticated musical cultures on Earth developed without any tuning system that could be called equal temperament.

## 6. Overall Assessment: What the Evidence Shows

### What the ET thesis gets RIGHT:
- ET did coincide with the most sustained and cumulative period of rhythmic innovation in Western history
- ET did remove harmonic resources (key character, pure intervals) that had previously carried expressive weight
- The post-ET era shows a different *kind* of rhythmic complexity — more structural, more notated, more cumulative

### What the ET thesis gets WRONG:
- ET is clearly not a **necessary condition** for rhythmic complexity (proven by Ars Subtilior, Ockeghem, Indian tala, African polyrhythm)
- Rhythmic complexity existed at extraordinary levels centuries before ET
- Non-ET musical traditions independently achieved comparable or superior rhythmic sophistication

### The Most Honest Synthesis:

**Equal temperament is neither necessary nor sufficient for rhythmic complexity.** What it may be is a **contributing accelerant** — one factor among many that made Western rhythmic innovation more sustained and cumulative by removing the possibility of vertical expressiveness through tuning.

The strongest version of the thesis is:

> *Equal temperament did not cause rhythmic complexity. It removed one of the reasons composers had previously not needed to rely on rhythm as heavily — the rich vertical expressiveness of tuned key-relationships. In doing so, it made rhythmic innovation more structurally persistent, but it did not create it.*

### Danger Rating for the Original Thesis:

🔴 **The Ars Subtilior is a serious problem.** Any version of the thesis that claims ET *caused* rhythmic complexity must explain why music of Nancarrow-level complexity existed in the 1390s under Pythagorean tuning.

🟡 **The Ockeghem precedent is a moderate problem.** It shows that compositional rhythmic virtuosity (as opposed to improvisational) can flourish without ET.

🟡 **The Indian/African comparisons are moderate problems.** They show that non-ET cultures develop extraordinary rhythmic sophistication, though of a different character than the Western post-ET tradition.

🟢 **The "accelerant" or "ceiling-removal" version survives** the counter-evidence and may actually be strengthened by it — the distinction between pre-ET complexity (reversible, localized) and post-ET complexity (persistent, cumulative) is a genuine and interesting pattern.

## 7. Recommendations for the Paper

1. **Lead with the counter-evidence.** A paper that opens by acknowledging the Ars Subtilior and Ockeghem will be far more credible than one that pretends they don't exist.

2. **Retreat from causation to contribution.** The evidence does not support "ET caused rhythmic complexity." It may support "ET contributed to the persistence and intensification of rhythmic complexity in the Western tradition."

3. **Distinguish types of complexity.** The Ars Subtilior's complexity (mensuration canons, proportional notation) is different in character from 20th-century complexity (polytempo, metric modulation, irrational rhythms). This distinction may be where the thesis has its strongest footing.

4. **Acknowledge the cycle hypothesis.** Even if ET is a contributing factor, the broader pattern of complexity oscillation needs to be addressed.

5. **Use the cross-cultural evidence carefully.** Indian and African traditions show that ET is not necessary for rhythmic sophistication, but the *type* of sophistication differs in ways that may still be relevant to the thesis.
