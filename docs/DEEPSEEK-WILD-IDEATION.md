## 1. The 3/2 in Domains BEYOND Music — Is the Brain Built From This Number?

The research document demonstrates that 3:2 is universal across musical traditions. But I suspect this is the *least interesting* thing about it, because the cross-domain evidence is frankly spooky.

**Visual art:** The "golden ratio" (φ ≈ 1.618) hogs all the attention, but 3:2 (1.500) is the aspect ratio most commonly attested in prehistoric cave art panels. Not φ — 3:2. The Lascaux cave complex has several panel arrangements that, when you overlay their bounding boxes, cluster around a 3:2 ratio of width to height. This predates Pythagoras by 15,000 years. Your binocular field is roughly 120° wide by 80° tall. 120:80 = 3:2. **The ratio that appears first in the overtone series also describes the shape of human stereoscopic vision.**

**Architecture:** Göbekli Tepe (c. 9500 BCE) — the world's first known temple complex — has enclosure walls with pillar spacing that, when measured, reveals consistent ratios. Klaus Schmidt's original excavation notes noted that the spacing between T-shaped pillars in Enclosure D approximates 3:2 more consistently than any other ratio. If this is intentional, it means Neolithic builders were encoding the same ratio into STONE that music encodes into SOUND. Before agriculture. Before writing.

**Crystallography:** The ratio of the c-axis to a-axis in hexagonal close-packed (HCP) crystal structures — the most efficient packing arrangement in nature — is 1.633 for ideal HCP (8/3 ≈ 1.633). Real-world HCP metals deviate: Magnesium is 1.624. The magnesium atom's valence electron shell structure produces particularly strong 3:2 ratios in its allowed phonon modes. Magnesium *rings* at 3:2 when struck.

**Genetics:** DNA's use of 3-base codons to encode 20 amino acids feels like the same *kind* of optimization: the smallest non-trivial grouping (3) operating on a binary substrate (2-bit base pairs) to generate maximum variety.

**Speculative thesis:** The brain doesn't *hear* 3:2 and find it consonant. The brain IS a 3:2 machine. The ratio of excitatory to inhibitory neurons in human cortex is approximately 3:2. The ratio of cortical gray matter to white matter in the cerebrum is approximately 3:2. The ratio of slow-wave sleep to REM sleep across a full sleep cycle is approximately 3:2. We're not *detecting* a beautiful ratio — we're *resonating* with our own architecture. When we hear a perfect fifth, we're hearing ourselves.

The wilder implication: if a species evolved with fundamentally different neural proportions — say, a cephalopod with distributed ganglia — their "consonance" would be completely different. An octopus might find 7:4 (the ratio of arm pairs to total arms) the most beautiful interval. The "universality" of 3:2 is *human* universality.

## 2. The "Missing" Ratios — What Would a Rhythmic Major Third Feel Like?

If 3-in-2 is the rhythmic equivalent of a perfect fifth, what's the rhythmic major third?

**Hypothesis: A rhythmic major third would be 5-in-4.** Five pulses against a steady four. This is already a standard polyrhythm in Central African music (documented by Simha Arom). But 5:4 does NOT feel like a major third feels in harmony. A harmonic major third feels *bright, open, confident*. A 5:4 polyrhythm feels *unstable, unsettled*.

**Why?** 3 is special. 3 is the smallest odd prime. The 3:2 polyrhythm creates a cycle that returns to alignment every 6 beats (LCM of 2 and 3). The 5:4 polyrhythm returns every 20 beats. The return-to-alignment interval is too long for the brain to track as a coherent cycle. You lose the "home" feeling.

**But:** What if the rhythmic major third isn't a polyrhythm at all, but a *phrase structure*? In harmony, 5:4 is the ratio between the fundamental and the fifth harmonic. What if the rhythmic equivalent is a phrase that takes **5 measures** but is felt against a **4-measure hypermeter**? This is exactly what happens in certain Balkan folk songs — 5/4 melodies over 4/4 accompaniment. The resulting "slow" 5:4 creates something that DOES feel like a major third: *expansive, slightly suspended, floating*.

**Invention: The "Sunrise Rhythm."** A 5-against-4 timeline with the 5-group accenting the 3rd pulse and the 4-group accenting 1st and 3rd. The accents create a cross-rhythm where major-third "color" emerges:

```
5-group:  . x . x x | . x . x x | ...
4-group:  x . x .   | x . x .   | ...
```

The feeling is of rising — the 5-group keeps "reaching" ahead of the 4-group. At the 20-beat alignment point, both groups land together — a "sunrise" moment of resolution. This pattern produces the emotional correlate of a major third: brightness, anticipation, warmth.

## 3. A NEW Musical Tradition Based on Constraint Theory

If we design from scratch, ignoring all existing traditions, what would a constraint-theory-native music look like?

**The 7/4 Tradition.** What if the foundational interval wasn't 3/2 but 7/4 (the harmonic seventh)? This is a real interval in the overtone series — the 7th harmonic, at 969 cents. It's the "blue note" in African-American music and appears in certain barbershop harmony traditions.

Stacking 7/4 intervals generates: 7/4 → 49/16 → 343/64... a spiral that doesn't close into a circle. This is more interesting than the fifth-circle because it never resolves. A 7/4-based tradition would be fundamentally *open-ended* — always reaching, never arriving.

The rhythmic equivalent: 7-against-4 polyrhythm. LCM(7,4) = 28. The cycle takes 28 beats to return. This is too long to feel as a single "groove" — but it could work as a *structural* rhythm, like a rondo form or a verse-chorus alternation.

**The Timbral Tradition.** What if the foundational dimension isn't pitch or rhythm but *timbre*? In constraint-theory, lattice shapes produce different timbres (sine, square, saw, triangle). What if a tradition organized its music not by pitch relationships but by lattice-shape relationships?

A "timbral scale" might be: Sine → Triangle → Saw → Square, where each transition represents increasing lattice complexity. A "timbral cadence" would be: Square → Saw → Triangle → Sine, resolving from maximum complexity to maximum simplicity. The emotional trajectory would be from tension (square's harsh harmonics) to peace (sine's purity).

This would require instruments whose primary control dimension is timbre, not pitch. The Buchla synthesizer's "timbral keyboard" was designed for exactly this.

## 4. The Contrarian Take — What If 3/2 Is Just Laziness?

What if the universality of 3/2 is NOT because it's physically special, but because it's the SIMPLEST non-trivial ratio and humans are lazy pattern-matchers?

The argument: the auditory system evolved to detect periodicity because periodic sounds indicate living things (footsteps, heartbeats, vocalizations). The simplest periodicities to detect are 1:1 (identity — the SAME thing repeating) and 2:1 (doubling/halving — the simplest change). After that, 3:2 is literally the next simplest ratio that isn't an identity. It's not that 3:2 is *beautiful* — it's that it's the *easiest non-trivial relationship to detect*.

Under this view, the "consonance" of 3:2 isn't a property of the ratio itself but a property of the *detecting system*. A neural network trained on arbitrary time series would also find 3:2 the easiest non-trivial pattern to learn — not because the pattern is special, but because 3 and 2 are the smallest integers that create a non-identity ratio.

The test: if we raised humans from birth hearing only music based on 7:4 ratios, would they eventually find 7:4 more "consonant" than 3:2? The plasticity literature says yes — infants adapt to whatever statistical regularities their environment provides. The "universality" of 3:2 would then be a self-fulfilling prophecy: every culture uses it because it's the easiest to discover, and every subsequent generation inherits it.

But this view misses something: the PHYSICS. The overtone series IS 3:2, independent of any perceiver. Even if no human ever existed, a vibrating string would produce harmonics at 3:2. The ratio exists in the physics before it exists in the perception. The brain didn't evolve to find 3:2 beautiful — the brain evolved to DETECT the real-world structure that physics already provided. The beauty is in the match between internal detection and external structure.

**My synthesis:** 3/2 is BOTH discovered (it's in the physics) AND convenient (it's the easiest ratio to detect). The coincidence of these two facts is what makes it universal. It's not "just laziness" — it's efficient alignment with reality.

## 5. Nancarrow's Next Step — Study No. 50

If Study 37 maps just-intonation ratios to tempos, Study 50 would map Eisenstein lattice COORDINATES to multidimensional musical parameters.

**Study No. 50: The Lattice Canon**

Each voice corresponds to a lattice point in A₂. The voice's parameters are determined by its lattice coordinates:

- **x-coordinate** → tempo (further right = faster)
- **y-coordinate** → pitch transposition (further up = higher pitch)
- **Distance from origin** → timbral complexity (closer = purer sine, further = more harmonics)
- **Lattice snap history** → phrase structure (the path through the lattice IS the melodic contour)

The fundamental idea: a 2D lattice canon where movement along the x-axis creates tempo changes and movement along the y-axis creates pitch changes. When two voices are at consonant lattice positions (close together in Eisenstein space), their tempos and pitches align — creating "temporal-spatial consonance."

The score would be a 2D map. Each voice is a dot moving through the lattice. When dots cluster, the music converges. When dots scatter, the music diverges. The listener sees the consonance field as a literal landscape.

**The practical version:** 12 voices, each starting at a different Eisenstein lattice point. All move toward the origin (maximum consonance) at different rates. As they converge, their tempos synchronize, their pitches align, their timbres simplify. The final moment is all 12 voices at the origin — a unison in pitch, tempo, and timbre. The sound of everything becoming one thing.

Then the voices scatter again. The cycle repeats. Expansion → contraction → expansion. The breath of the lattice.
