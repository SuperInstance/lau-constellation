## I. THE VERTICAL 3/2 — Perfect Fifth as Universal Foundation

### The Phylogenetic Interval

The perfect fifth (3:2) is not a cultural invention. It's a **physical discovery**. When a string vibrates, its second harmonic — the first overtone beyond the octave — sounds at 3:2 the fundamental. This isn't Western theory. It's physics. Every ear that has ever heard a resonating body has heard 3:2.

**The traditions that found it independently:**

**古琴 (Gǔqín) — China (~3000 BCE):**
The Chinese chromatic system 十二律 (shí'èr lǜ, twelve pitch-pipes) was generated by the **三分损益法** (sān fēn sǔn yì fǎ) — "subtract three, add one" method. Multiply a string length by 2/3 → you go up a fifth. Multiply by 4/3 → you go down a fourth (inverted fifth). The entire 12-tone gamut is generated by repeatedly applying 3:2 and 4:3. Ling Lun (伶伦), the mythological founder of Chinese music, was said to have cut bamboo pipes using these ratios at the command of the Yellow Emperor. The 五声 (wǔ shēng, five notes) — 宮商角徵羽 (gōng shāng jué zhǐ yǔ) — are all derivable from cycling 3:2.

**वीणा (Vīṇā) — India (~2000 BCE):**
Bharata's Nāṭya Śāstra describes 22 śruti (microtonal positions) within the octave. The first and fifth scale degrees — सा (Sa) and प (Pa) — are always in a 3:2 relationship. Sa and Pa are called **अचल (achala)** — immovable. Every raga, every mode, every ornament in Indian classical music has Sa-Pa as its skeleton. The tambūrā drone that begins every classical performance sounds nothing but Sa and Pa (and sometimes the octave Sa). Three strings tuned to Sa, one to Pa. The entire edifice of rāga rests on the perpetual audible presence of 3:2.

**العود (Al-ʿūd) — Arabic world (~800 CE, roots in Mesopotamia):**
Al-Fārābī's *Kitāb al-Mūsīqā al-Kabīr* (Grand Book of Music) describes the ʿūd's fretting using Pythagorean ratios. The fifth finger position on the ʿūd produces a 3:2 interval. Yet here's the crucial difference: **Arabic music does not DEPEND on the fifth** in the same way Western music does. Many maqāmāt in the Kurd family have their غمّاز (ghammāz, secondary tonal center) on the FOURTH (4:3), not the fifth. The fifth is present but not always sovereign. This is important — it means the fifth is a SUFFICIENT condition for musical organization, but not always NECESSARY.

**Mbira / Dzavadzimu — Shona people, Zimbabwe:**
The mbira dzavadzimu is tuned in heptatonic scales, but the tuning system ensures that adjacent tines produce intervals based on simple ratios including 3:2. The instrument's resonator (deze) amplifies harmonics that emphasize the fifth. When two mbira players perform together (as in traditional ceremonies), they naturally create interlocking patterns at 3:2 temporal ratios — the same number, vertical AND horizontal simultaneously.

**Pythagoras — Greece (~500 BCE):**
The legend: Pythagoras walked past a blacksmith's shop, heard hammers producing consonant sounds, investigated, and discovered that hammer weights in ratio 12:9:8:6 produced the octave (2:1), fifth (3:2), and fourth (4:3). The story is probably apocryphal — weight ratios don't produce pitch ratios — but the mathematical insight was real. Pythagoras or his followers used the monochord to demonstrate that dividing a string in ratio 3:2 produces the most consonant interval after the octave. This became the foundation of **Pythagorean tuning**, where the entire 12-tone chromatic scale is generated by stacking 3:2 fifths.

### The Universal Sangam

The perfect fifth is the **one interval** where every tradition agrees. Our consonance analysis confirms this computationally:

```
Universal Sangam (6 traditions tested):
  3/2 → consonance 0.275 (highest shared consonance)

Bhairavi ↔ Hijaz: 30.4 cents average deviation (musical neighbors!)
Bhairavi ↔ Blues: 64.3 cents average deviation
Major    ↔ Hijaz: 23.6 cents average deviation
```

The fifth is the **phoneme** of music — like how every human language has some version of the "ah" vowel. It precedes culture. It's the acoustic substrate on which cultures build their differences.

## II. THE HORIZONTAL 3/2 — When the Fifth Becomes Time

### Hemiola: The Rhythm That Is the Interval

**ἡμιόλιος** originally meant ONLY the pitch ratio 3:2. The Pythagoreans used it for the fifth. The application to rhythm — three equal durations in the space of two — emerged in the **15th century**, a full two millennia after the term was coined.

But here's what's extraordinary: **the rhythmic 3:2 existed in practice long before it was named.**

**Africa — The Timeline (пєдгα):**
In sub-Saharan African music, the bell pattern (the "timeline") often creates a 3-against-2 polyrhythm as the foundational rhythmic cell. This isn't hemiola as a temporary displacement — it's the PERMANENT rhythmic ground. The 3:2 polyrhythm is described as the **"heartbeat"** of African music. The Ewe people of Ghana have a saying: "If you can feel 3 against 2, you can feel anything."

The bell pattern for the Ewe Agbekor rhythm:
```
x . . x . x . . x . x . x . . x
1   2   3   4   5   6   7   8
```
Grouped as 3+3+2 — which is **8/8 felt as 3+3+2** — the asymmetry of a prime group (3) inside a composite (8 = 2×2×2). The 3 fights the 2. The tension IS the music.

**India — Tāla and the 3-in-2 Principle:**
Rūpaka tāla: 3+2+2 = 7 beats. The 3 leads.
Jhaptāla: 2+3+2+3 = 10 beats. The 3 interleaves.
Khaṇḍa jāti: 5 beats as 4+1 or 3+2.

The ādi tāla (8 beats, 4+2+2) is considered the "father" of all tālas in Carnatic music. But even here, the internal grouping 4+2+2 can be felt as **two groups of 2 + one group of 4**, which is itself a 1:2 ratio — a compression of the 3:2 principle into metric structure.

The konnakoḷ (vocal percussion) tradition takes this further. A skilled mridangam player will play patterns in 3 over a tāla of 8 — creating a 3-in-2 feel. When the pattern resolves back to the sam (beat 1), the emotional effect is one of **return** — like a musical sentence that wanders through strange territory and arrives home.

**Balkans — Aksak: "The Limping Rhythm":**
Aksak (Turkish: "limping") is the asymmetric meter tradition of the Balkans and Turkey. The principle is simple: **every asymmetric meter is composed of 2s and 3s**.

7/8 → 2+2+3 or 3+2+2 or 2+3+2
9/8 → 2+2+2+3 or 3+2+2+2
11/8 → 3+3+3+2

Notice: the 3 always appears. You can't build an interesting asymmetric meter from only 2s. The 3 is the **spice**, the deviation from regularity that creates groove. Without the 3, you just have duple meter with more beats.

Bartók documented these rhythms extensively in his folk music collections, calling them "Bulgarian rhythms." He incorporated them into works like the Fifth String Quartet and the Concerto for Orchestra, introducing asymmetric meters to the Western art music tradition.

**Greece — The Emotional Mathematics:**
The ancient Greeks classified rhythms by their **Arsis** (upbeat) and **Thesis** (downbeat) patterns. Even numbers of metrical feet were associated with certain emotional qualities:

- **Trochaic** (2 beats: long-short) — marching, purposeful
- **Iambic** (2 beats: short-long) — speechlike, natural
- **Dactylic** (3 beats: long-short-short) — epic, noble
- **Anapestic** (3 beats: short-short-long) — rushing, excited

The Greek insight that Casey references — that **even numbers of groups of 3** create emotional empathy — relates to this: 3 beats per group, 2 groups per phrase = 6 beats. The brain recognizes the 3-group and the 2-group simultaneously. You feel both. The cognitive polyvalence IS the emotion.

### The Prime Division Principle

When a time signature has a prime numerator, the body automatically searches for a grouping pattern. And across all traditions, the grouping follows the same principle:

**Divide the prime into groups of 2 and 3, with 3s appearing as close to equally-spaced as possible.**

7 → 2+2+3 or 3+2+2 (one 3, as central as possible)
11 → 3+3+3+2 (three 3s, one 2)
13 → 3+3+3+2+2 or 3+3+2+3+2
5 → 3+2 or 2+3

This isn't convention. It's **embodied cognition**. The human perceptual system naturally groups rhythmic input into chunks of 2-4 beats. A group of 3 is the smallest unit that creates asymmetry. When you hear a 7-beat pattern, your brain tries to fit it into 2-beat groups, but one beat is left over. The leftover creates a "lean" — the aksak, the "limp." The 3-group is where the lean resolves.

This is EXACTLY analogous to the harmonic situation: the perfect fifth (3/2) is the smallest consonant ratio that creates asymmetry. 2/1 (octave) is identity. 3/2 is the first departure from identity that still sounds resolved. In rhythm, a group of 3 is the smallest grouping that departs from the duple "heartbeat" pattern while still feeling natural.

**3/2 vertical: the smallest interesting consonance.**
**3/2 horizontal: the smallest interesting asymmetry.**
**Same ratio. Different domain. Same feeling.**

## III. SACRED 3, SECULAR 2 — Medieval Europe's Rhythmic Theology

### Tempus Perfectum and Tempus Imperfectum

In medieval music theory (13th-14th centuries), rhythm was organized by **mensural notation** — measured time. The fundamental division was:

- **Tempus perfectum** (perfect time): the semibreve divides into **3** minims. Symbol of the Trinity. Sacred.
- **Tempus imperfectum** (imperfect time): the semibreve divides into **2** minims. Human, earthly. Secular.

This wasn't just notation — it was **theology encoded in rhythm**. The number 3 represented the Holy Trinity (Father, Son, Holy Spirit). The number 2 represented duality, the flesh, the mundane. To compose in triple meter was to invoke the divine. To compose in duple was to invoke the human.

The **Notre Dame school** (Léonin, Pérotin, c. 1160-1250) developed the first systematic rhythmic notation in Western music. Their **rhythmic modes** — six patterns of long and short durations — were almost exclusively in triple meter. The **Magnus Liber Organi** (Great Book of Organum) is overwhelmingly in tempus perfectum.

### The Emotional Weight of Triple Meter

Why did triple meter feel sacred? Beyond the Trinitarian symbolism, there's a perceptual truth:

**Triple meter creates a cycle that "returns."**
Beat 1 (strong) → Beat 2 (weak) → Beat 3 (weak) → Beat 1 (strong again).

The third beat creates **expectation**. After beat 2, the listener knows beat 3 is coming, and after beat 3, the listener knows beat 1 is coming. This creates a sense of inevitability, of **fate** — appropriate for sacred music that speaks of divine order.

**Duple meter creates forward motion.**
Beat 1 → Beat 2 → Beat 1 → Beat 2.

No third beat to create that "rounding" feeling. The rhythm moves forward relentlessly. Appropriate for dance, for march, for the worldly.

This emotional asymmetry between 3 and 2 in meter is the SAME emotional asymmetry between the perfect fifth (3/2) and the perfect fourth (4/3 = 2²/3 × 2). The fifth has an upward pull — it wants to resolve UP to the octave. The fourth has a downward pull — it wants to resolve DOWN to the unison. In medieval theory, the fifth was the **hierarchical superior**.

### D major = Sacred, C major = Simple

Before equal temperament, **each key had a different acoustic color** because of meantone temperament. In quarter-comma meantone (the most common system from ~1500-1750):

- The fifths were narrowed by 1/4 of the syntonic comma (~5.4 cents each)
- This made the major thirds PURE (5:4 ratio, zero beating)
- But only in keys close to the home key
- Remote keys had increasingly dissonant thirds — the "wolf intervals"

**D major** had a particularly bright, resonant quality because:
1. D is the open string of the violin (the dominant orchestral instrument)
2. The trumpet's natural harmonic series is in D
3. In meantone, D major's third (F♯) was one of the better-tuned major thirds
4. The overall effect was brilliance, festivity, **glory**

This is why Handel's "Hallelujah" Chorus is in D major. Why Bach uses D major for the most celebratory movements of the Mass in B minor. Why Haydn's "festive" symphonies are disproportionately in D.

**C major** was considered "pure" and "natural" — not because of any acoustic superiority, but because it was the **reference point** of the tuning system. In meantone, C major's intervals were among the most pure. Its simplicity (no sharps or flats) made it the starting point for music education — hence its association with children's songs and innocence.

The Affektenlehre (Doctrine of Affections) codified these associations:
- D major: triumph, glory, martial joy, sacred celebration
- C major: innocence, purity, simplicity, childlikeness
- G minor: tragedy, sorrow, seriousness
- E♭ major: nobility, warmth
- F minor: grief, deep suffering

**These weren't arbitrary — they were ACOUSTIC REALITIES.** In meantone temperament, each key literally sounded different. The "color" of a key wasn't metaphor. It was physics.

## IV. THE POLYPHONY CRISIS — When the Church Almost Banned Harmony

### Docta Sanctorum Patrum (1324/5)

Pope John XXII's papal bull **Docta sanctorum patrum** didn't ban polyphony outright — it restrained its excesses. But the threat was real enough that composers took notice.

The specific grievances:
- **Hocket** (hiccup): rapid alternation of sound and silence between voices, creating a choppy, "indecent" texture
- **Motets with secular texts**: sacred melodies buried under layers of French love poetry
- **Complex rhythm**: semibreves and minims (very fast notes) obscuring the chant
- **Discantus**: note-against-note counterpoint that prioritized vertical consonance over textual clarity

The bull prescribed: simple note-against-note consonances (octaves, fifths, unisons) on feast days. The chant must remain recognizable. Rhythm must not distract from the sacred text.

### The Composers' Instinctive Mathematics

Here's what's remarkable: the composers who developed polyphony — Léonin, Pérotin, Marchetto da Padova, Philippe de Vitry — **didn't know the mathematical ratios underlying their intuitions**. Or rather, they knew SOME ratios (Pythagorean tuning was known), but they didn't have the mathematical vocabulary to describe what they were doing.

Marchetto da Padova (~1318) described the "chromatic genus" using divisions of the whole tone that weren't mathematically precise but were **perceptually accurate**. He heard something — a certain quality of interval in certain progressions — and tried to notate it. His divisions of the tone into 2, 3, 4, or 5 parts anticipated equal temperament by two centuries.

The Notre Dame composers developed **modal rhythm** — the six rhythmic modes — which were essentially **algorithms for generating rhythmic patterns**. They didn't think of it that way. They thought of it as: "this pattern sounds like a dance, this one sounds like a prayer, this one sounds like a procession."

But the MATH was there, underneath:
- Mode 1 (trochaic): long-short | ratio 2:1 — the simplest non-trivial ratio
- Mode 3 (dactylic): long-short-short | ratio 3:1+1 — the 3 appears
- The **perfect long** was worth 3 breve units. The **imperfect long** was worth 2.

3 and 2 again. Always 3 and 2. The composers felt it. They didn't calculate it.

### How Composers "Figured Out" the Math Without Numbers

The constraint-theory insight applies here: **consonance is a field, and composers navigated it by ear.** They didn't need to know that 5/4 (major third) was more consonant than 81/64 (Pythagorean third). They could HEAR the difference. In meantone temperament, the pure major third had **no beating** — it was still, serene. The Pythagorean third had audible beats — it was restless, tense.

Composers learned to:
1. Place pure intervals at moments of repose (cadences, pedal points)
2. Place dissonant intervals at moments of tension (passing notes, suspensions)
3. Resolve tension toward consonance — ALWAYS toward consonance

This is the **gradient of the consonance field** — exactly what our `ConsonanceField.gradient_at()` computes. The composers were doing gradient descent on a landscape they couldn't see but could hear.

## V. EQUAL TEMPERAMENT — "Everything Sounds Equally Bad"

### The Great Flattening

Equal temperament (ET) divides the octave into 12 exactly equal semitones. Each semitone is 100 cents. Each fifth is 700 cents (instead of 702 cents in just intonation). Each major third is 400 cents (instead of 386 cents in just intonation).

The price:
- **Fifths**: slightly flat (2 cents narrow). Acceptable.
- **Fourth**: slightly sharp (2 cents wide). Acceptable.
- **Major thirds**: 14 cents too wide. **Painful to sensitive ears.**
- **Minor thirds**: 16 cents too narrow. Dull.
- **All keys sound identical.** The Affektenlehre dies. Key color dies.

The gain:
- **Unlimited modulation.** You can go from any key to any key.
- **Enharmonic equivalence.** G♯ = A♭. One note, two names. Simple.
- **Mechanical compatibility.** Fretted instruments (lute, guitar) naturally play in ET.
- **Industrialization.** Pianos can be mass-tuned. Orchestras can play together.

The complaint — "everything sounds equally bad" — was real and widespread. Giuseppe Tartini (1692-1770) called ET "un musicidio" (a music-killing). The philosopher Rousseau argued that ET destroyed the emotional character of keys. Many string players continued to adjust their intonation toward just intervals for centuries after ET became standard.

### How Folk Music Preserved the Colors

While the art music world was flattening itself into ET, **folk music preserved the old tunings** through fretted instruments.

Lutes and guitars in the Renaissance and Baroque eras were often fretted in **meantone or just intonation** — the frets were placed for specific keys, and the instrument couldn't modulate freely but sounded SWEET in its home keys. When a folk guitarist played in G major, the major third (B) was a genuine 5:4 ratio, not the 400-cent compromise of ET.

This is why folk music from the 18th and 19th centuries has a quality that art music of the same period doesn't: **it still has key color.** The guitar is a folk instrument. The piano is an industrial instrument. The guitar preserves the older, more beautiful tuning. The piano enforces the newer, more democratic one.

### The Piano as Democracy Machine

The piano's adoption of ET wasn't just a tuning decision — it was a **social revolution**. ET made it possible for:
- A household piano to play any song in any key
- Music to be published in standardized notation
- Music education to be systematized around 12 equal semitones

But the cost was the **loss of vertical color** — the specific acoustic quality of each key. In meantone, D major didn't just FEEL triumphant — it SOUNDED triumphant, because its intervals were physically brighter. In ET, D major "triumphant" is a cultural convention, a memory of a sound that no longer exists physically.

### The Rhythmic Compensation

Here's Casey's key insight: **when vertical (harmonic) color was flattened by ET, composers compensated with horizontal (rhythmic) complexity.**

The logic:
1. In meantone, each key had unique harmonic color → composers could create interest through harmony alone
2. ET removed harmonic color → all keys sound the same → harmonic interest diminished
3. Composers needed a new source of interest → **rhythm became the new frontier**

This isn't just theoretical. Look at the timeline:
- **Renaissance (~1450-1600)**: meantone tuning, modal harmony, simple rhythms. Interest is VERTICAL.
- **Baroque (~1600-1750)**: transition to well-temperament, increasingly complex harmony, rhythm still relatively simple. VERTICAL dominance continues but strains.
- **Classical (~1750-1820)**: ET begins to dominate. Haydn and Mozart develop the sonata form — which is essentially a DRAMATIC structure of key relationships. But the key relationships are becoming metaphorical, not acoustic.
- **Romantic (~1820-1900)**: ET is standard. Chopin, Liszt, Wagner push harmony to its breaking point. But also: **rhythmic innovation accelerates**. Chopin's rubato, Brahms's hemiolas, Dvořák's Slavic rhythms, Liszt's Hungarian/Roma-influenced asymmetric meters.
- **Modern (~1900-present)**: ET is universal. Stravinsky's *Rite of Spring* (1913) is a RHYTHMIC revolution. The harmonies are dissonant, but it's the RHYTHM that caused riots. Nancarrow abandons performers entirely and writes for player piano — rhythm without limit.

The pattern is clear: **as vertical color flattened, horizontal complexity compensated.**

## VI. THE PLAYER PIANO — Where Horizontal Became the Only Dimension

### Conlon Nancarrow's Horizontal Revolution

Nancarrow (1912-1997) was a composer who, frustrated by the limitations of human performers, turned to the player piano as his sole medium. His insight was radical:

**If the piano (in ET) has already killed harmonic color, and if performers limit rhythmic complexity, then the player piano — which CAN'T produce dynamic variation but CAN execute any rhythm with perfect precision — is the ideal instrument for exploring the horizontal dimension.**

The player piano has no vertical dynamics. A note is either on or off. There's no touch sensitivity, no crescendo, no timbral variation. The ONLY parameter is TIME.

### Study No. 37 — Just Intonation in Rhythm

Study No. 37 is Nancarrow's masterpiece of the pitch-rhythm analogy. It's a 12-voice canon where each voice moves at a different tempo, and the tempo ratios correspond EXACTLY to the ratios of a justly-tuned chromatic scale:

```
Voice  1: MM 150      = 1/1   (unison)
Voice  2: MM 160 5/7  = 15/14 (semitone)
Voice  3: MM 168 3/4  = 9/8   (major second)
Voice  4: MM 180      = 6/5   (minor third)
Voice  5: MM 187 1/2  = 5/4   (major third)
Voice  6: MM 200      = 4/3   (perfect fourth)
Voice  7: MM 210      = 7/5   (tritone)
Voice  8: MM 225      = 3/2   (PERFECT FIFTH)
Voice  9: MM 240      = 8/5   (minor sixth)
Voice 10: MM 250      = 5/3   (major sixth)
Voice 11: MM 262 1/2  = 7/4   (minor seventh)
Voice 12: MM 281 1/4  = 15/8  (major seventh)
```

**Voice 8 moves at a tempo ratio of 3/2 — the perfect fifth — relative to Voice 1.**

In this piece, the vertical 3/2 (harmonic interval) and the horizontal 3/2 (rhythmic ratio) are THE SAME THING. The perfect fifth exists simultaneously as:
- A pitch relationship (the melody each voice plays)
- A tempo relationship (the speed at which each voice moves)
- A structural relationship (when voices align in time)

Nancarrow discovered what Cowell had theorized in *New Musical Resources* (1930): **the overtone series generates both pitch and rhythm.** 3:2 is the same structural principle whether it operates vertically (harmony) or horizontally (tempo).

### Henry Cowell's Rhythmicon (1930)

Cowell, working with Leon Theremin, built the **Rhythmicon** — the first rhythm synthesizer. Each of its 16 keys produced:
- A pitch at a given frequency
- A rhythm at the corresponding rhythmic ratio

Key 1: fundamental pitch, 1 pulse per measure
Key 2: pitch at 2:1, 2 pulses per measure
Key 3: pitch at 3:2, 3 pulses per measure (the fifth!)
Key 5: pitch at 5:4, 5 pulses per measure (the major third!)

The 3:2 key produces the SAME ratio in pitch and rhythm simultaneously. The Rhythmicon was the first instrument to make the vertical-horizontal equivalence physical.

## VII. THE DEEP RELATIONSHIP — 3/2 as the Single Generating Principle

### The Isomorphism

```
VERTICAL (Harmony)              HORIZONTAL (Rhythm)
─────────────────────           ─────────────────────
3/2 = perfect fifth             3-in-2 = hemiola
Most consonant non-identity     Most groovy non-identity
Foundation of tonal hierarchy   Foundation of rhythmic tension
Sa-Pa in Indian music           3+2+2 in rūpaka tāla
Gōng-zhǐ in Chinese music       3+2 in gǔqín rhythm
Dominant in Western harmony     Hemiola in Brahms waltzes
Tower in organ tuning           Asymmetric meter in Bartók

Same ratio. Same feeling. Different domain.
```

### Why 3/2 Specifically?

The ratio 3:2 occupies a **unique position** in the landscape of possible ratios:

1. **Simplest non-identity consonance**: 1:1 (unison) and 2:1 (octave) are identities — they're the "same note." 3:2 is the first ratio that produces a DIFFERENT note while remaining perfectly consonant.

2. **Symmetric between 2 and 3**: It contains both the duple principle (2) and the triple principle (3). It bridges the two fundamental grouping patterns.

3. **Hearing it feels like "returning"**: In harmonic terms, the fifth resolves to the octave (3:2 × 4:3 = 2:1). In rhythmic terms, 3-in-2 resolves when the pattern lands on beat 1 again. Both create a sensation of **completion after departure**.

4. **It generates everything**: The Pythagorean circle of fifths generates all 12 chromatic pitches. The rhythmic equivalents generate all asymmetric meters. One ratio, recursively applied, produces the entire tonal AND rhythmic vocabulary of most world traditions.

### The Constraint-Theory Perspective

In our lattice framework, the perfect fifth corresponds to the lattice vector (1, 0) in A₂ — the shortest non-zero vector. It's the **unit step** of the Eisenstein lattice. Every other interval is a combination of steps in the (1, 0) and (0, 1) directions.

The perfect fifth is to the Eisenstein lattice what the number 1 is to the integers: the **generator** from which everything else is composed.

And in rhythm, 3-in-2 is to time what the perfect fifth is to pitch: the **basic unit of interesting tension**. Every polyrhythm is built from combinations of 2s and 3s. Every asymmetric meter is 2s and 3s. The 3-in-2 is the atom of rhythmic interest.
