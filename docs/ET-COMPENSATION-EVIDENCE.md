# Equal Temperament and Rhythmic Compensation: A Chronological Evidence Document

**Thesis:** Equal Temperament killed harmonic key-color, which forced composers to compensate with rhythmic complexity.

**Status:** Research document — compiled 2026-05-24. Claims rated by evidence strength.

---

## Phase 1: Before ET (pre-1700) — The Age of Vertical Color

### 1.1 Tuning Systems in Actual Use

#### Quarter-Comma Meantone (dominant c.1490–1700)

**First documented:** Pietro Aaron, *Thoscanello de la musica* (1523) — described quarter-comma meantone though didn't notate all 12 pitches.

**How it worked:**
- 11 of 12 perfect fifths narrowed to ~696.6 cents (pure = 702 cents, ET = 700 cents)
- Major thirds tuned to pure 5:4 ratio = 386.3 cents (ET = 400 cents — a **13.7 cent deviation**)
- This produced 8 usable major triads and 8 usable minor triads out of 12 possible

**Key cents chart (1/4-comma meantone, from Owen Jorgensen, *Tuning*, 1991):**

| Pitch | Cents from C |
|-------|-------------|
| C     | 0.0         |
| C#    | 76.0        |
| D     | 193.2       |
| Eb    | 310.3       |
| E     | 386.3       |
| F     | 503.4       |
| F#    | 579.5       |
| G     | 696.8       |
| G#    | 772.6       |
| A     | 889.7       |
| Bb    | 1006.8      |
| B     | 1082.9      |

Note the **unequal semitones**: C-C# = 76 cents, C#-D = 117 cents, D-Eb = 117 cents. Each key truly sounded *different* — not metaphorically, but acoustically.

#### Specific Composers and Their Tunings

**Girolamo Frescobaldi (1583–1643):** Composed in quarter-comma meantone environment. His keyboard works exploit meantone's chromatic expressiveness. The *cembalo cromatico* (an instrument with split keys for enharmonic distinction) was associated with his circle, suggesting he was pushing against meantone's boundaries from *within* the system. His *Fiori musicali* (1635) stays within meantone-viable keys.

**Johann Jakob Froberger (1616–1667):** Studied with Frescobaldi in Rome (1637–1641). Composed within meantone sound-world. His tombeaux and lamentations exploit meantone dissonances for rhetorical drama. Modern HIP (historically informed performance) recordings use quarter-comma meantone for his works.

**Heinrich Schütz (1585–1672):** Primarily vocal composer; vocal ensembles can adjust intonation dynamically. However, his continuo keyboard parts were played in meantone, and his harmonic language reflects this constraint — he stays within the 8+8 usable triad universe.

**Dieterich Buxtehude (c.1637–1707):** Transitional figure. Active in Lübeck, likely used a modified meantone or early well-temperament. His organ works venture further around the circle of fifths than earlier composers, suggesting tuning was already loosening.

### 1.2 The Wolf Interval — Acoustic Reality

**The wolf fifth in quarter-comma meantone:** The residual 12th fifth (typically G#-Eb or equivalently Ab-D#) measures **~737.6 cents** — a **+35.6 cent deviation** from a pure fifth (702 cents) or **+37.6 cents from ET fifth** (700 cents).

**Keys rendered unplayable:**
- **F# major** — major third F#-A# = 427 cents (41 cents wider than pure — audibly wretched)
- **Ab major** — contains the wolf fifth Ab-Eb
- **B major** — major third B-D# = 427 cents
- **Db major** — major third Db-F = 427 cents

**Evidence from repertoire:** Kyle Gann notes: "Look through a 16th- or 17th-century keyboard collection, such as the Fitzwilliam Virginal Book. You won't find a piece in a key signature of more than two sharps or flats." Orlando Gibbons's *Lord Salisbury Pavane* (c.1610) in A minor uses F, G, C, E, and D major triads but **never** F# major (V/ii), because it literally did not exist on his instrument.

### 1.3 Key CHARACTER as Acoustic Reality — Primary Sources

#### Johann Mattheson, *Das neu-eröffnete Orchestre* (1713)

Mattheson assigned specific emotional characters to **17 major and minor keys** in Part III, Chapter II. Key examples:

| Key | Mattheson's Character |
|-----|----------------------|
| C major | "Rude and impudent character; suited to rejoicing" |
| C minor | "Extremely lovely, but sad" |
| Eb major | "Very full of pathos; never serious or plaintive or indeed exuberant" |
| F major | "Magnanimity, firmness, perseverance, love, virtue, ease" |
| A minor | "Sumptuous and serious look... quiet, even inviting to sleep" |

**Crucial context:** Mattheson was describing **acoustic reality**, not mere convention. In meantone or well-temperament, C major literally sounded different from Eb major because the interval ratios were physically different. This is NOT the same as modern "key color" folklore (where people claim F# major sounds "green" in ET — it doesn't, because in ET all keys are identical).

#### Jean-Jacques Rousseau, *Dictionnaire de musique* (1767)

Rousseau described the *tempérament ordinaire* (French ordinary temperament, based on D'Alembert's 1752 system), noting:
- Central keys (near C major) had **pure thirds** and consonant harmony
- Sharp keys were **favored** — relatively smooth
- Flat keys were **progressively harsher** — the three fifths on the flat side of C were slightly wide, making major thirds above F, Bb, Eb progressively worse
- He explicitly noted that "less common, transposed keys provide great resources for more pronounced expression"

This is a theorist directly stating that key character = acoustic tuning consequence.

#### Johann David Heinichen, *Der General-Bass in der Composition* (1728)

Heinichen's "Musicalischer Circul" (Musical Circle) was an early circle of fifths diagram. His discussion of keys presumed unequal temperament — the circle was not yet "closed" in practice because distant keys sounded dramatically different. Heinichen's thorough-bass treatise was written for composers navigating these acoustic differences.

### 1.4 Key Choice as Acoustic Decision — Evidence

1. **Frescobaldi's *Fiori musicali* (1635):** All pieces in meantone-friendly keys (C, D, F, G, A minor, etc.). The choice of key was not arbitrary — it determined which harmonies were available.

2. **Gibbons's *Lord Salisbury Pavane* (c.1610):** As Kyle Gann documented, the piece in A minor is shaped by which triads exist. Gibbons goes to F, G, C, E, and D major — all available in meantone. He never touches F# major. "Had Gibbons begun in the key of C minor, he would have had to write a different piece, because instead of moving from A minor to F major, he would have had to move from C minor to Ab major, and Ab major, strictly speaking, didn't exist on his harpsichord."

3. **Schütz's avoidance of remote keys:** His concerted sacred works stay within meantone-viable harmonic territory, with keyboard continuo constraints shaping the harmonic language.

4. **The Fitzwilliam Virginal Book (c.1620):** Survey confirms near-zero pieces in signatures beyond 2 sharps/flats. Key = acoustic decision.

## Phase 2: The Transition (1700–1800) — ET Creeps In

### 2.1 When Did ET Actually Become Common?

**Short answer: Much later than commonly assumed. Exact ET was not achievable on pianos until 1917.**

#### Timeline of Key Temperaments:

**Andreas Werckmeister III (1691):**
- Published in *Musicalische Temperatur*
- A well-temperament, NOT equal temperament
- 4 fifths narrowed by 1/4 Pythagorean comma (C-G, G-D, D-A, B-F#); remaining fifths pure
- Cent deviations from ET (relative to C=0):
  - C: 0, C#: -3.9, D: +3.9, Eb: 0, E: -3.9, F: +3.9, F#: 0, G: +2.0, G#: -7.8, A: 0, Bb: +2.0, B: -2.0
- **Key consequence:** Keys near C sound pure and "sweet"; distant keys sound progressively sharper and more tense. All 24 keys are *playable*, but each has distinct character.

**Johann Philipp Kirnberger III (1779):**
- Published in *Die Kunst des reinen Satzes*
- Kirnberger studied with J.S. Bach (1739–1741) and claimed his temperament reflected Bach's preferences
- 10 pure fifths; comma concentrated on 2 fifths (D-A narrowed by full syntonic comma, E-B adjusted)
- Near-pure thirds in C, G, D, A major
- **Still distinctly unequal** — each key has unique color

**Piano manufacturer standardization:**
- Kyle Gann: "Equal temperament... is pretty much a 20th-century phenomenon"
- During the 19th century, keyboard tuning **drifted** closer to ET "over the protest of many of the more sensitive musicians"
- **No exact method for tuning ET on piano existed until 1917** (William Braid White's system)
- Before 1917, tuners approximated; the result was usually a de facto well-temperament
- Broadwood and Steinway were transitioning through the mid-to-late 19th century, but the results were approximate

### 2.2 Bach's *Well-Tempered Clavier* — Was It ET?

**Current scholarship says NO.**

**Bradley Lehman's 2005 analysis** (*Early Music*, Feb & May 2005: "Bach's Extraordinary Temperament: Our Rosetta Stone"):

- Interpreted a decorative "squiggle" on the title page of Bach's autograph manuscript as a tuning diagram
- When viewed upside-down, the squiggle represents a chain of fifths with varying tempering
- Three plain circles = pure fifths; different loop shapes = different tempering amounts
- Result: a specific **unequal temperament** giving each of the 24 keys unique character
- **Controversy:** Some scholars accept it; others (notably O'Donnell 2006, via Piano Technicians Guild) argue the squiggle is merely decorative. Other manuscript copies omit the design or have different patterns.

**What we can say with confidence:**
1. Bach titled it "Well-Tempered" — in his era, this meant "usable in all keys," NOT "equal in all keys"
2. Bach once tormented the organ builder Gottfried Silbermann by playing sour Ab-major triads on one of Silbermann's meantone organs — indicating he *wanted* to use all keys and was frustrated when he couldn't
3. The WTC exploits key character differences for expressive effect — the C# major Prelude sounds different from the C major Prelude not just because of notation, but because the *intervals themselves were different sizes*

### 2.3 Semitone Cents Comparison Across Systems

| Interval | Meantone (1/4c) | Werckmeister III | Kirnberger III | Equal Temperament |
|----------|-----------------|------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| C-C#     | 76.0            | 96.1             | ~90            | 100              |
| C#-D     | 117.2           | 103.9            | ~110           | 100              |
| C-E (3rd)| 386.3 (pure!)   | ~392             | ~388           | 400 (+13.7¢ off) |
| C-G (5th)| 696.8           | ~698             | ~702 (pure)    | 700              |
| Wolf 5th | 737.6 (!!)      | N/A (no wolf)    | N/A (no wolf)  | N/A              |

**The key insight:** In meantone, the difference between the smallest semitone (76¢) and largest (117¢) is **41 cents** — nearly half a semitone. In Werckmeister III, the range narrows but is still audible (~7-8 cents variation). In ET, all semitones are exactly 100 cents — **zero variation.**

### 2.4 Complaints About Loss of Key Character

1. **Rousseau (1767):** Noted that Couperin had rejected equal temperament. Rousseau himself preferred the *tempérament ordinaire* because of its varied key character, recognizing that ET offered "uniformity" at the cost of expressiveness.

2. **Kirnberger (1779):** Actively advocated for unequal temperament as superior to ET. His student Marpurg promoted ET; Kirnberger fought back, claiming ET destroyed the expressive differences between keys.

3. **19th-century resistance:** Kyle Gann documents that "the consensus was that [ET] sounded awful: out of tune and characterless." The drift toward ET happened despite, not because of, aesthetic preference.

4. **Hermann von Helmholtz, *On the Sensations of Tone* (1863, English translation 1875 by Alexander J. Ellis):** Helmholtz documented the acoustic superiority of just intonation and the compromises of ET. He noted that ET major thirds (400¢) are significantly wider than pure (386¢), creating audible beats. While Helmholtz was a scientist, not a complainer, his work provided the acoustic evidence that ET thirds were objectively less consonant.

## Phase 3: The Compensation (1800–present) — Horizontal Complexity Rises

### 3.1 Beethoven: Syncopation and Rhythmic Disruption

**Specific works showing rhythmic escalation:**

- **Symphony No. 3, *Eroica* (1804):** The scherzo features driving rhythmic ostinati and abrupt metrical shifts unprecedented in Haydn or Mozart's symphonies.
- **Symphony No. 5 (1808):** The famous opening motif (short-short-short-LONG) is a rhythmic, not melodic, idea. The entire first movement develops this rhythmic cell.
- **Symphony No. 7 (1812):** Wagner called it "the apotheosis of the dance." The second movement (Allegretto) builds an entire structure from a repeated rhythmic ostinato — a technique more associated with later composers.
- **Grosse Fuge, Op. 133 (1825):** Late Beethoven's rhythmic language approaches modernity — jagged cross-rhythms, abrupt tempo changes, rhythmic fragmentation of subject.
- **String Quartet No. 14, Op. 131 (1826):** Seven movements played without break; continuous tempo modulation creates a through-composed rhythmic arc.

**Quantitative note:** While rigorous statistical comparisons of syncopation rates between Haydn and Beethoven are not widely published in standardized form, the qualitative shift is well-documented in analytical literature. Beethoven's late works contain dramatically more syncopation, hemiola, and cross-accent than equivalent Haydn works. See especially the work of William Rothstein (*Phrase Rhythm in Tonal Music*, 1989) and Joel Lester (*The Rhythms of Tonal Music*, 1986).

### 3.2 Chopin: Rubato and Cross-Rhythm

**Rubato:**
- Chopin's *tempo rubato* = melodic flexibility (RH) over steady accompaniment (LH)
- Liszt described it as "a tempo agitated, broken, interrupted, a movement flexible, yet at the same time abrupt and languishing"
- **Nocturne Op. 9 No. 2:** Rubato used to emphasize expressive repetitions
- **Nocturne Op. 15 No. 3:** Marked "Languido e rubato" at opening — explicit instruction

**Cross-rhythms and polyrhythms:**
- **Étude Op. 10 No. 10:** "A veritable hornets' nest of cross-rhythms and syncopations" — alternating triplets and duplets
- **Fantaisie-Impromptu, Op. 66 (1834):** Classic 3-against-4 polyrhythm throughout
- **Nocturne Op. 9 No. 1 (c.1832):** Contains a **22-note group** in RH against steady LH — extreme rhythmic independence
- **Nocturne Op. 27 No. 2 (1835):** 7:6 polyrhythmic relationship between hands
- **Ballade No. 4 in F minor, Op. 52 (1842):** Contains 7:4 and 5:3 polyrhythms; climactic passage has three simultaneous rhythmic layers creating hemiola dislocation

**Note:** Chopin is an interesting test case for the thesis — he was famously attached to meantone-like tuning on his Pleyel piano and reportedly insisted that each key had its own character. He may represent a composer trying to *preserve* vertical color while simultaneously pushing horizontal complexity.

### 3.3 Brahms: Hemiola Density

**Specific evidence of elevated hemiola usage:**

- Brahms is the textbook case for pervasive hemiola in music analysis pedagogy
- His music features "endless" hemiolas, including: double hemiola, reverse hemiola, displaced hemiola
- Rhythmic instability via "syncopations, overlapping cross-rhythms and hemiolas" is a defining characteristic of his style
- **Symphony No. 3, Op. 90 (1883):** Third movement contains layered hemiolas creating metrical ambiguity
- **Symphony No. 4, Op. 98 (1885):** Finale (passacaglia) builds rhythmic complexity through variations with progressive hemiola overlay
- **Intermezzo Op. 117 (1892):** Late piano works are saturated with 3-against-2 patterns and metric displacement
- **Academic sources:** See especially Harald Krebs, *Fantasy Pieces: Metrical Dissonance in the Music of Robert Schumann* (Oxford, 1999) — while focused on Schumann, establishes the analytical framework applied to Brahms. Also Yonatan Malin, *Songs in Motion: Rhythm and Meter in the German Lied* (Oxford, 2010).

**No rigorous published count exists** comparing "hemiolas per measure" between Brahms and Mozart. The qualitative difference is overwhelming and universally acknowledged in analytical literature, but the thesis would benefit from a systematic count if someone were to undertake one.

### 3.4 Liszt: Asymmetric and Changing Meters

Liszt's rhythmic innovations were less about asymmetric time signatures per se (those came later with Stravinsky) and more about:

- **Metrical ambiguity through written-out rubato** — passages where the notation effectively creates changing meter
- **Dante Sonata (Après une lecture du Dante) (c.1849):** Features driving rhythmic ostinati with irregular accent patterns
- **Faust Symphony (1857):** Three movements depicting characters through distinctive rhythmic profiles; the Gretchen movement uses lyrical rubato and cross-rhythm
- **Hungarian Rhapsodies (1851–1886):** The *lassan* (slow) sections feature free rubato; the *friska* sections drive with rhythmic energy and syncopation patterns derived from Roma music
- **Note:** Liszt did not typically use actual asymmetric meters (5/4, 7/8) — those came with early 20th-century composers. His contribution was the liberation of rhythm from regular phrase structure and the use of *written-out tempo modification* as structural element.

### 3.5 Stravinsky: *The Rite of Spring* (1913)

**Quantified rhythmic innovations:**

- **Constant meter changes:** "Dance of the Adolescents" alternates between 2/4, 3/4, and 5/8. First two pages show meter changes in nearly every measure.
- **Final "Sacrificial Dance":** A different meter in practically every measure
- **Irregular signatures used:** 2/16, 3/16, 4/16, 5/16, 2/8, 3/8, 5/8, 7/8
- **Polyrhythms:** Rehearsal 7, 2nd measure — piccolos play quintuplets while cellos/bassoons play triplets simultaneously
- **Displaced accents:** "Dances of the Young Girls" (Rehearsal 13) — French horns place *sforzandos* on the "ands" of beats 1 and 2
- **Rhythmic layering:** Different orchestral sections play contradictory rhythms simultaneously
- **Ostinato as structural principle:** Repeated rhythmic cells serve as anchors in otherwise chaotic textures
- **Joseph Straus** identifies 5 basic rhythm/meter types in the work, ranging from rapidly changing meters to rigidly unvarying pulses

### 3.6 Nancarrow: Tempo Complexity as Horizontal Extremes

**Conlon Nancarrow, *Studies for Player Piano* (1948–1992):**

- 49 studies over 4 decades
- **Tempo canons** — voices at different, precisely calculated speeds:
  - Study No. 15: Diverging-converging canon, ratio 3:4
  - Study No. 27: Acceleration canon — 8 voices accelerating/decelerating at varying rates over constant bass
  - Study No. 36: Four-part canon 17/18/19/20 — each voice at fixed tempos 85, 90, 95, 100
  - Study No. 37: 12-part canon with tempo ratios corresponding to chromatic scale vibrations (Nancarrow considered this his most important work)
  - Study No. 40: Irrational ratio *e*:*π*
  - Study No. 48C: Double canon with tempo proportion 60:61 — near the limit of perceptual discrimination
- **Study No. 25:** 12-second section with **1,028 notes**, sustain pedal down — up to **200 notes/second**
- **Timeline:** Studies 1–30 (1948–1960) explored fixed-ratio canons; Studies 31–41 (1965–1977) developed accelerating/irrational canons; Studies 42–49 (1980s–1992) pushed to extreme complexity
- **Key fact:** Nancarrow used player piano specifically because the rhythmic complexity exceeded human performance capability — the horizontal dimension had become so dominant it required a machine

### 3.7 Minimalism: Different Horizontal Strategy (1960s–present)

Steve Reich, Philip Glass, Terry Riley represent a *different* kind of horizontal complexity:

- **Steve Reich:** *Piano Phase* (1967), *Drumming* (1971) — phase-shifting where identical patterns played at slightly different speeds create gradually evolving textures. *The Desert Music* (1984) uses shifting meters. *Different Trains* (1988) layers speech-derived rhythms.
- **Philip Glass:** Additive process — expanding/contracting rhythmic cells (e.g., *Music in Twelve Parts*, 1971–1974). Not "complex" in the Nancarrow sense, but horizontal process entirely replaces harmonic color as the primary structural element.
- **Terry Riley:** *In C* (1964) — 53 melodic fragments, each performer decides when to advance. The resultant texture is a complex, emergent rhythmic layer that no single individual controls.

**Minimalism is actually STRONG evidence for the thesis:** These composers abandoned harmonic verticality (and its key-color implications) entirely, replacing it with horizontal process as the *sole* structural driver. The fact that this happened *after* ET had fully homogenized vertical color is not coincidental.

## Phase 4: The Counter-Evidence

### 4.1 Rhythmically Complex Composers BEFORE ET

This is the most serious challenge to the thesis.

#### Ars Subtilior (c.1380–1415)

- **The elephant in the room.** Ars subtilior ("subtler art") is a musical style centered on Avignon, Paris, and northern Spain at the end of the 14th century.
- Characterized by **extreme rhythmic and notational complexity** — "not until the twentieth century did music again reach the most subtle refinements and rhythmic complexities" (Richard Hoppin, *Medieval Music*, 1978)
- **Key composers:** Baude Cordier, Jacob Senleches, Philipoctus de Caserta, Solage, Antonio Zacara da Teramo
- **Specific works:**
  - Cordier's *Tout par compas* — perpetual canon notated on a circular staff
  - Solage's *Fumeux fume par fumée* — extreme chromaticism and rhythmic complexity
  - Senleches's *La Harpe de melodie* — notated in the shape of a harp; rhythmically intricate
- **Used mensural notation with extreme subdivision** — semibreves, minims, semiminims, and complex proportional canons

**Why this challenges the thesis:** Ars subtilior predates meantone's dominance, let alone ET. Its rhythmic complexity was driven by *notational sport* and intellectual virtuosity among a small courtly elite, not by any tuning compensation.

**Mitigation:** Ars subtilior was a brief, localized phenomenon (c.35 years) that died out completely and had no continuous influence on later practice. It is an exception, not a counter-trend.

#### Johannes Ockeghem (c.1410–1497)

- **Missa Prolationum:** A mensuration canon where all four voices sing the same notated line at different proportional speeds simultaneously
- **Missa cuiusvis toni:** Can be sung in any mode — a compositional puzzle, not a tuning statement
- Complex proportional canons that predate any tuning crisis

**Mitigation:** Ockeghem's complexity is *contrapuntal/proportional*, not rhythmic in the modern sense of syncopation and cross-accent. It's a different species of complexity.

### 4.2 Did Some Composers Get SIMPLER Rhythmically After ET?

- **Saint-Saëns (1835–1921):** Deliberately classical in rhythmic orientation; rejected Wagnerian and modernist complexity. His music is often rhythmically straightforward even by Classical-era standards.
- **Fauré (1845–1924):** Rhythmically subtle but not complex — flowing, regular meter with gentle rubato. Less rhythmically adventurous than Chopin, who preceded him.
- **Erik Satie (1866–1925):** Deliberately anti-complex. *Vexations* (1893) is a short motif repeated 840 times — extreme in duration, but the *rhythmic material itself* is simple. *Gymnopédies* (1888) are rhythmically minimal.
- **Philip Glass (b.1937):** While his music is "complex" in aggregate (layered patterns), the individual rhythmic cells are extremely simple. The complexity is *additive*, not *intrinsic*.

**This actually supports a modified version of the thesis:** The trend toward horizontal complexity is a *statistical tendency*, not a universal law. Some composers moved in the opposite direction — and notably, many of these "simplifiers" were also the ones most explicitly reacting against Romantic complexity.

### 4.3 Causation vs. Correlation — The Hardest Question

**Possible third factors that could explain BOTH ET adoption and rhythmic complexity:**

1. **Industrialization and standardization:** The 19th century saw standardization in everything (railroad gauges, time zones, screw threads). ET is the "standardized screw thread" of music — part of a broader cultural trend. Rhythmic complexity might be a reaction against industrialization's homogenization, not specifically against ET's homogenization.

2. **Larger orchestras and venues:** As performance spaces grew (from court chambers to public concert halls), vertical subtlety (like meantone thirds) became harder to hear at distance. Composers may have shifted to rhythmic complexity because it *projects* better in large spaces — a practical, not aesthetic, motivation.

3. **The breakdown of tonality itself:** ET enabled modulation to any key, which enabled chromaticism, which led to the dissolution of functional tonality (Wagner → Schoenberg). Rhythmic complexity may be a response to *tonal uncertainty*, not to *tuning homogenization* per se. Once you can't rely on harmonic function for structure, rhythm becomes the structural backbone.

4. **Notational capacity:** As music notation became more sophisticated (more precise rhythmic notation, metronome marks after 1815), composers could *specify* more complex rhythms. Some of the apparent increase in rhythmic complexity may be an artifact of notation catching up with compositional imagination.

5. **The separation of composer and performer:** In the Baroque era, composers typically performed their own works. As a professional class of interpreters emerged, composers could write rhythms they couldn't necessarily play themselves, trusting virtuosos to execute them.

**The honest assessment:** The correlation between ET adoption and increased rhythmic complexity is **strong and chronologically consistent**, but **causation is not definitively established** and likely involves multiple interacting factors. The thesis is *plausible* and *supported by circumstantial evidence*, but it would be more accurately stated as:

> **"Equal temperament eliminated the vertical dimension of key-color that had been a primary expressive resource for centuries. Composers working in the ET paradigm, whether consciously or not, developed horizontal (rhythmic) complexity as one of several strategies to compensate for the lost dimension of expression."**

## Summary of Evidence Strength

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Notes |
|-------|------------------|-------|
| Pre-ET tuning systems produced distinct key colors | **STRONG** | Acoustic measurements, primary sources (Mattheson, Rousseau), repertoire constraints |
| Composers chose keys for acoustic reasons | **STRONG** | Meantone repertoire analysis, Gibbons case study, Fitzwilliam survey |
| ET adoption was late (19th-20th c., not Bach's era) | **STRONG** | Jorgensen, Gann, Lehman; 1917 first exact ET tuning method |
| Key color was lost with ET | **STRONG** | Acoustic fact: ET = 100¢ per semitone, zero variation |
| Rhythmic complexity increased over 19th-20th centuries | **STRONG** | Documented in Beethoven, Chopin, Brahms, Stravinsky, Nancarrow |
| The increase is *caused by* ET killing key color | **MODERATE** | Correlation strong; causation plausible but confounded by multiple factors |
| Ars subtilior is a counter-example (rhythmic complexity pre-ET) | **MODERATE** | Real but localized/ephemeral phenomenon; different species of complexity |
| Minimalism supports the thesis (horizontal replaces vertical) | **MODERATE** | Consistent with thesis but could have independent motivations |
