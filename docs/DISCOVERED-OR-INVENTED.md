# Is 3/2 Discovered or Invented?
## *An Essay on the Ontological Status of Music's Deepest Ratio*

> अचल (achala) — immovable. The śruti that cannot be moved.
> The Sanskrit word for what the West calls the perfect fifth says everything.
> Not chosen. Not constructed. *Immovable.*

---

## Prologue: The Question That Haunts Every Tradition

Somewhere around 500 BCE, a Greek philosopher held a monochord and heard something that stopped him mid-thought. When he divided the string in the ratio 3:2, the sound was — there is no other word for it — *right*. Not pleasing in the way a well-cooked meal is pleasing, a matter of taste and culture, but right in the way a triangle's angles sum to 180 degrees. The consonance felt like recognition, not invention. As though the ear were built for this ratio, had been waiting for it.

Two thousand years earlier and three thousand miles east, someone — we don't know who — had already found the same ratio in bamboo pipes. The Chinese chromatic system, generated by the 三分损益法 (sān fēn sǔn yì fǎ, "subtract three, add one" method), is built on cycling 3:2 and 4:3. The myth credits Ling Lun (伶伦), who cut bamboo at the command of the Yellow Emperor. But whether the finder was a sage or an anonymous craftsman, the ratio was the same. The fifth was already there, waiting in the physics of the pipe, the same way it waited in the physics of the Greek monochord.

In India, the Nāṭya Śāstra names 22 śruti within the octave, but only two are called अचल (achala) — immovable: सा (Sa) and प (Pa). The fifth. It is the skeleton of every rāga, sounded perpetually by the tambūrā drone — three strings tuned to Sa, one to Pa. The entire universe of Indian melody exists in the space between these two fixed points, and the relationship between them is always, necessarily, immutably 3:2.

So: did these traditions discover the same mathematical object, or did they independently invent similar musical conventions? Is 3/2 in the territory, or only in the map?

The answer, I will argue, is that the question is malformed — or rather, that it reveals something profound about the relationship between mathematics, physics, and perception. The ratio 3:2 is not purely discovered (like a planet) or purely invented (like chess). It is something stranger: a *revelatory constraint* — a structure that emerges from the physics of the universe and is then recognized by any nervous system sophisticated enough to process periodic signals. It is discovered in the same sense that the periodic table is discovered: the underlying structure is mind-independent, but the *significance* of the structure — its musical meaning, its emotional weight — requires a perceiver.

Let us walk through the evidence.

## I. The Pythagorean Argument: Mathematics as Eternal Truth

The Pythagorean school held that number is the arche — the fundamental substance of reality. "All is number," said Pythagoras, and he meant it literally. The harmony of the spheres (ἁρμονία τῶν σφαιρῶν) was not metaphor: the planets moved in ratios, and these ratios were the same ones that produced musical consonance.

For the Pythagoreans, 3:2 was not invented. It was *revealed* through the monochord, the way a geometer reveals a theorem that was already true. The perfect fifth is a mathematical object that exists independent of human minds, in the same category as prime numbers or the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter. Before any human plucked a string, before any ear existed to hear it, the second harmonic of any vibrating body stood in 3:2 relation to the fundamental. This is physics, not culture.

The strength of the Pythagorean position: 3:2 is *physically inevitable*. Given any vibrating string, column of air, or resonating body, the overtone series is determined by the wave equation. The fundamental frequency f produces overtones at 2f, 3f, 4f, 5f, and so on. The ratio between the second harmonic (2f) and the third harmonic (3f) is always 3:2. This is not a convention. It is a solution to the wave equation with fixed boundary conditions. It would hold on any planet, in any universe with the same physics of vibration.

The Pythagorean weakness: the move from physics to *music* requires a perceiver. The overtone series is physical; the *judgment* that 3:2 sounds consonant is perceptual. The Pythagoreans elided this distinction. They assumed that because 3:2 is mathematically simple, it must be perceptually privileged. But mathematical simplicity and perceptual salience are not the same thing — or are they?

This is where it gets interesting.

---

## II. The Indian Position: Śruti as Dharma — Discovered Law

The Indian musical tradition offers a subtler metaphysics than the Pythagorean one. The 22 śruti are not arbitrary divisions of the octave. They are positions within a continuum that is itself determined by the physics of the drone. When the tambūrā sounds Sa and Pa simultaneously, the combination produces combination tones, difference tones, and reinforcement patterns that *highlight* certain positions within the octave and *obscure* others. The śruti are, in a sense, already present in the acoustic field of the drone, waiting to be articulated.

The word श्रुति (śruti) itself means "that which is heard" — from the root श्रु (śru), to hear. The śruti are not made; they are heard. The tradition holds that the ancient ṛṣi (seers) *heard* the śruti the way one hears a river — the river was there before you arrived. This is a discovery ontology: the śruti are features of the acoustic landscape, not constructions of the human mind.

But the Indian tradition also contains a counter-current. Bharata's Nāṭya Śāstra describes the 22 śruti as arising from a specific tuning procedure — cycling fifths and fourths, the same procedure the Chinese used. This procedure *generates* the śruti; it does not merely reveal them. The śruti are the output of an algorithm applied to the octave. Are they properties of the octave itself, or properties of the algorithm?

The answer depends on whether the algorithm is *natural* or *arbitrary*. If cycling 3:2 is the only reasonable way to divide the octave — if it is, so to speak, forced by the physics of vibration — then the śruti are discovered. If there are equally valid alternative procedures that produce different divisions, then the śruti are at least partly constructed.

The evidence favors discovery. The 3:2 cycle generates a chromatic gamut that closely matches the overtone series. It is not the only possible division (the Arabic tradition uses 24-quarter-tone divisions; some Turkish traditions use 53-tone commas), but it is the division that most naturally arises from the physics of periodic vibration. The śruti are discovered in the same way that the chemical elements are discovered: the raw materials are determined by physics, but the *naming* and *categorizing* are cultural.

The designation of Sa and Pa as अचल (achala, immovable) is the Indian tradition's way of saying: this is not negotiable. This is bedrock. You can move other pitches, ornament them, bend them, slide between them — but the fifth is fixed. It is the axis on which the entire system turns.

## III. The Chinese Question: Did Ling Lun Invent or Discover?

The myth of Ling Lun (伶伦) is instructive. The Yellow Emperor commands Ling Lun to create a system of music. Ling Lun travels to a valley in the western mountains, cuts bamboo pipes, and uses the 三分损益法 (sān fēn sǔn yì fǎ) to generate the twelve pitches (十二律, shí'èr lǜ). The method: multiply a pipe's length by 2/3 to go up a fifth, by 4/3 to go down a fourth. Repeat twelve times and you return (approximately) to the starting pitch, having visited all twelve chromatic positions.

The question: did Ling Lun *invent* the 三分损益法, or did he *discover* it?

If you cut a bamboo pipe and then cut another pipe at 2/3 its length, the shorter pipe produces a pitch at 3:2 ratio to the longer one. This is a physical fact about resonating air columns. Ling Lun didn't invent it. But the *decision to keep applying this ratio*, to generate a complete chromatic system from it — that is an act of mathematical imagination. The ratio is discovered; the algorithm is invented; the system that results is a collaboration between physics and mind.

The Chinese tradition seems to understand this intuitively. The five tones of the pentatonic scale — 宮商角徵羽 (gōng shāng jué zhǐ yǔ) — are given cosmological significance: they correspond to the five elements (五行, wǔ xíng) — earth, metal, wood, fire, water. This mapping is cultural, constructed, *invented*. But the acoustic relationship between the tones — the fact that they can be derived from cycling 3:2 — is not. The Chinese tradition layers cultural meaning on top of physical discovery, and it knows the difference. The pitch-pipes generate the pitches; the cosmology generates the meaning.

This is, I think, the correct framework. 3:2 is discovered as a physical fact; the musical system built from it is invented as a cultural artifact. But the discovery *constrains* the invention so tightly that, across independently developed traditions, the resulting systems look remarkably similar. Chinese 五声, Indian śruti, Greek tetrachords, Arabic maqāmāt, African tuning systems — all of them, despite vast cultural differences, build on the same ratio. The convergence is too precise to be coincidence. It is the convergence of minds encountering the same physical reality.

## IV. The African Argument: Is 3-Against-2 Pre-Human?

If the vertical 3:2 (the perfect fifth) is a feature of vibrating bodies, the horizontal 3:2 (three-against-two polyrhythm) is a feature of *time itself*. The Ewe people of Ghana describe 3-against-2 as the "heartbeat" of music. The saying — "If you can feel 3 against 2, you can feel anything" — suggests that this polyrhythm is not merely conventional but *foundational*: the simplest rhythmic cell from which all others are built.

Is 3-against-2 pre-human? The question is not as outlandish as it sounds. Rhythmic entrainment — the ability to synchronize movement to a periodic pulse — has been demonstrated in several non-human species. Snowball the cockatoo can synchronize to a musical beat (Patel et al., 2009). Chimpanzees drum on buttress roots and show rhythmic entrainment in social contexts (Fuhrmann et al., 2014). Bonobos have been observed spontaneously synchronizing drumming with human partners (Large & Gray, 2015).

But entrainment to a *beat* is not the same as processing *polyrhythm*. The 3-against-2 pattern requires the ability to maintain two conflicting periodicities simultaneously and to perceive their relationship as meaningful tension rather than noise. This is a more sophisticated cognitive operation.

The neuroscientific evidence, however, is suggestive. Bouncing experiments with infants (Phillips-Silver & Trainor, 2005) show that even pre-verbal babies can encode meter — they prefer to listen to rhythms that match the meter they were bounced to. And the 3-against-2 polyrhythm is the simplest polyrhythm there is: the lowest-numbered pair of primes. If any polyrhythm is cognitively primary, it is this one.

The evolutionary argument: if rhythmic entrainment is a byproduct of social bonding (as proposed by Merker, 2000, and supported by Dunbar's work on grooming and endorphin release), then the simplest polyrhythm — 3:2 — would be the first polyrhythm to emerge in any species that develops rhythmic coordination. It is the entry point into polyrhythmic space, just as 3:2 is the entry point into harmonic consonance. The two entry points are the same number. This cannot be coincidence.

I predict that if we test non-human primates on polyrhythmic discrimination — can a chimp distinguish 3:2 from 4:3? — the 3:2 pattern will show the strongest neural response and the most consistent behavioral discrimination. The ratio 3:2 is not culturally learned. It is the fingerprint of a nervous system that processes periodic signals.

## V. The Quantum Question: Is 3/2 an Eigenvalue?

In quantum mechanics, the energy levels of a vibrating string are quantized: Eₙ = (n + ½)ℏω, where n is the quantum number and ω is the fundamental frequency. The allowed frequencies are f, 2f, 3f, 4f, and so on — the harmonic series. The ratio 3:2 appears as the relationship between the second and third energy eigenstates.

Is this significant, or merely a mathematical coincidence? I think it is significant. The eigenstates of the vibrating string are determined by the boundary conditions (fixed endpoints) and the wave equation. These are not human constructions; they are features of the physical universe. The ratio 3:2 emerges from the eigenvalue structure of the simplest possible vibrating system.

In the language of quantum mechanics, 3:2 is not an eigenvalue itself but the *ratio of two consecutive eigenvalues* (E₃/E₂ = 3/2, neglecting zero-point energy, or more precisely, the frequency ratio of the third and second modes). This ratio is determined by the geometry of the system. For a one-dimensional string with fixed endpoints, the eigenvalues are integers: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5... The ratio 3:2 falls out of this sequence inevitably.

Compare this to electron orbitals. The quantum numbers (n, l, m, s) determine the electron's energy, shape, and spin. These are not invented; they are features of the Schrödinger equation for a Coulomb potential. The orbital structure of atoms is determined by physics, not convention. In the same way, the harmonic series — and the 3:2 ratio within it — is determined by the physics of vibration, not by musical convention.

The implication: any physical system that supports standing waves will exhibit the 3:2 ratio in its eigenvalue structure. This includes vibrating strings, resonating air columns, drumheads (for the radially symmetric modes), plasma oscillations, and even gravitational waves in certain geometries. The ratio 3:2 is not a feature of *music*. It is a feature of *waves*. Music merely exploits it.

## VI. The Neuroscience of Resolution

Why does the perfect fifth feel "resolved"? The psychoacoustic literature offers several explanations, all of which point toward a neural mechanism.

**Periodicity theory:** The auditory nerve encodes sound as a series of neural spikes timed to the waveform's peaks. For a 3:2 interval — say, 200 Hz and 300 Hz — the spike trains from the two frequencies have a common period at 100 Hz (the greatest common divisor). The auditory cortex can extract this common periodicity easily, and neurons in the brainstem (specifically the dorsal nucleus of the lateral lemniscus and the inferior colliculus) show phase-locking to this common period (Langner, 2015). The brain literally contains a neural oscillator that locks onto the 3:2 periodicity.

**Harmonic Template theory:** Terhardt (1974) and later McLachlan et al. (2013) proposed that the auditory system has an innate template for the harmonic series, against which incoming sounds are matched. A 3:2 interval fits this template perfectly (it is the relationship between harmonics 2 and 3). The brain recognizes it as "harmonically coherent" — a sound that could plausibly come from a single source. This recognition produces a sensation of consonance: the feeling that the interval is "unified" rather than "clashing."

**Neural resonance theory:** Large & Snyder (2009) proposed that rhythm perception relies on neural oscillators that entrain to periodic stimuli. These oscillators have preferred frequencies related by small-integer ratios. A 3:2 polyrhythm entrains two oscillators simultaneously — one at the duple frequency, one at the triple frequency — and the interaction of these oscillators produces a stable standing-wave pattern in neural activity. The "groove" of 3-against-2 is the feeling of these two neural oscillators locking into a stable relationship.

The convergence of these three theories is striking. At the level of the auditory nerve, the brainstem, and the cortex, 3:2 is processed as a "special" ratio — one that produces maximal neural coherence with minimal computational effort. The brain doesn't merely *prefer* 3:2; it is *built to process it efficiently*. The consonance of the fifth is not a cultural convention layered onto a neutral acoustic signal. It is a perceptual reflex, as automatic and as cross-cultural as the startle response.

## VII. The Linguistic Analogy: Is 3/2 a Phoneme?

Every human language contains the vowel /a/. Not because the languages are related (they aren't — this is a universal, not a cognate), but because /a/ is the simplest vowel to produce and to perceive. The oral tract in its most open configuration produces a formant pattern that maximizes acoustic energy transmission. Any vocal tract, regardless of its owner's linguistic community, will produce /a/ when opened fully.

Is 3:2 the /a/ of music? The analogy is precise. Every musical tradition contains the 3:2 interval. Not because the traditions are related (some are, some aren't — the convergence is too widespread to be explained by diffusion alone), but because 3:2 is the simplest consonance to produce and to perceive. Any vibrating system, regardless of its maker's cultural community, will produce a 3:2 relationship between its second and third harmonics.

The linguistic universalist Peter Ladefoged argued that phoneme inventories are constrained by the acoustics of the vocal tract. There are only so many distinguishable vowel qualities, and /a/ is the most acoustically salient. In the same way, there are only so many consonant intervals, and 3:2 is the most salient. The parallel extends further: just as languages can differ wildly in their phoneme inventories while all containing /a/ (English has ~44 phonemes; Rotokas has ~11; !Xóõ has ~160 — but all have /a/), musical traditions can differ wildly in their pitch systems while all containing 3:2 (Western music has 12 equal-tempered pitches; Indian music has 22 śruti; Arabic music has 24 quarter-tones; Thai music has 7 equal divisions — but all contain the fifth).

The implication: 3:2 is a universal feature of all possible musical languages, in the same way that /a/ is a universal feature of all possible spoken languages. It is not culturally transmitted; it is *structurally inevitable*. Any system that processes periodic signals will converge on 3:2 as the primary non-identity relationship.

The Japanese musicologist Fumio Koizumi noted something remarkable about Japanese folk songs: despite Japan's isolation from the Pythagorean, Indian, and Chinese theoretical traditions for long stretches of its history, the fundamental intervals of Japanese folk melody are overwhelmingly based on 3:2-derived pentatonic scales (the yo-na-nuki scale, 音抜き音階). The tradition didn't import the fifth from China — or if it did, it had already independently arrived at the same ratio through oral tradition before the theoretical contact. The fifth is structurally inevitable; the theory merely describes it.

## VIII. The Arabic Nuance: When the Fifth Is Present but Not Sovereign

The Arabic musical tradition offers an important counterpoint (if you'll forgive the pun). In the maqām system, the fifth is always *available* — al-Fārābī's *Kitāb al-Mūsīqā al-Kabīr* describes the ʿūd's fretting using Pythagorean ratios, and the fifth finger position produces a 3:2 interval. But in many maqāmāt — particularly those in the Kurd and Hijaz families — the secondary tonal center (غمّاز, ghammāz) is on the *fourth* (4:3), not the fifth. The fifth is present but not always sovereign.

This is crucial because it shows that 3:2 is a *sufficient* condition for musical organization but not always a *necessary* one. The Arabic tradition can build perfectly functional, emotionally rich musical structures around 4:3 (the inverted fifth, or equivalently, 3:2 read downward instead of upward). The fact that 4:3 is the mathematical complement of 3:2 (they multiply to give 2:1, the octave) means that even when the fifth is not the primary structuring interval, it is the shadow behind the fourth. You can't use 4:3 without implying 3:2, because the octave (2:1) is always present as the frame.

The Arabic tradition thus reveals the ontological status of 3:2 with unusual clarity. It is not an arbitrary choice — maqāmāt that avoid the fifth as primary center still use it extensively. It is not culturally relative — it appears in every maqām system from Morocco to Iraq. But it is also not an absolute dictator — musical traditions can organize themselves around other intervals while still acknowledging the fifth's gravitational pull. 3:2 is like the sun in a solar system: you can look away from it, but you can't escape its gravity.

## IX. The Philosophical Conclusion: Territory, Not Map

The question "Is 3/2 discovered or invented?" is analogous to the question "Is prime numbers discovered or invented?" Most mathematicians are Platonists about prime numbers: they are discovered, not invented. The primality of 7 was true before any mind existed to verify it. Similarly, the 3:2 ratio between the second and third harmonics of any vibrating body was true before any ear existed to hear it.

But music is not mathematics. The 3:2 *ratio* is discovered (it is physics); the 3:2 *interval* — the perfect fifth, the pa, the gōng-zhǐ — is a *perceptual category* that requires a nervous system to instantiate. The brain's auditory processing mechanisms are evolved structures, shaped by natural selection to efficiently process the sounds that occur in the natural world. Since the harmonic series is ubiquitous in nature (vocalizations, wind, impacts, resonance), any auditory system will be tuned to the intervals within it — and 3:2, as the simplest non-identity ratio in the series, will be the most privileged.

So the full answer: **3/2 is discovered as physics, perceived as biology, and codified as culture.** The territory contains the ratio. The map contains the name. The mind is the instrument that translates between them. The feeling of consonance — the "rightness" that Pythagoras heard, that the ṛṣi heard, that Ling Lun heard, that every mbira player and gamelan tuner and blues guitarist has heard — is the feeling of a nervous system successfully matching an incoming signal to its internal template. It is the feeling of recognition. And what is recognized is not invented. It was there all along.

To put it in the language of the Indian tradition: the śruti are श्रुति — *that which is heard*. They are not created by hearing. They are *revealed* by hearing. The ear does not make the fifth. The ear *finds* it. The fifth is in the bamboo, in the string, in the air column, in the overtone series of any sound that has ever resonated. It was in the first sound the universe ever made — the cosmic microwave background, if you could listen to it, would contain 3:2 ratios between its harmonic modes.

3/2 is in the territory. It is one of the deepest features of the territory. And every map that has ever been drawn — pentatonic, heptatonic, chromatic, microtonal — has traced this feature, because it cannot be ignored. It is the shape of vibration itself.

## X. The Prediction: What Would Alien Music Use?

If 3:2 is a feature of the territory rather than the map — if it emerges from the physics of vibration and the biology of periodic-signal processing rather than from human culture — then any civilization capable of making music will use 3:2 as its foundational ratio. Including civilizations that have no contact with Earth, no knowledge of human culture, and possibly no auditory system recognizable as homologous to ours.

**Prediction:** An alien civilization's music will use 3:2 as its most consonant non-identity interval, for the following reasons:

1. **Physics:** Any physical system that supports standing waves — strings, columns, membranes, plasma, electromagnetic cavities — will exhibit the harmonic series. The ratio between the second and third modes is always 3:2. This is not Earth-specific; it is geometry-specific.

2. **Information theory:** Any information-processing system that encodes periodic signals will discover that the simplest non-trivial relationship between two periodicities is 3:2. This follows from number theory: 3 and 2 are the two smallest primes, and their ratio is the simplest non-identity rational number.

3. **Evolutionary convergence:** If alien life evolves in an environment with resonating phenomena (which is to say, in any environment with matter and energy), any sensory system that processes acoustic or vibrational information will be shaped by natural selection to efficiently encode the harmonic series. The 3:2 ratio will be the most efficiently encoded non-identity interval.

4. **The principle of sufficient simplicity:** Even an intelligence radically unlike ours — a distributed intelligence, a silicon-based intelligence, a plasma-based intelligence — will, if it develops a symbolic system for representing periodic relationships, begin with the simplest ratios: 1:1 (identity), 2:1 (octave), and 3:2 (the fifth). These are not cultural choices; they are the first three entries in the Stern-Brocot tree, the Farey sequence, or any other systematic enumeration of rational numbers.

I will go further: not only will alien music use 3:2, but alien *rhythm* will use 3-against-2 as its foundational polyrhythm. The isomorphism between vertical and horizontal 3:2 — between the perfect fifth and the hemiola — is not a coincidence. It reflects a deeper truth: that 3:2 is the simplest relationship that creates *difference within coherence*. It is the boundary between identity (1:1, 2:1) and complexity (4:3, 5:4, 6:5...). Any system that processes periodic signals — in any domain, for any purpose — will encounter this boundary and will use it as the foundation of its organizational structure.

The Arabic philosopher al-Kindī wrote in the 9th century that "music is the proportion (نسبة, nisba) of all things." He meant that music is not merely sound organized in time, but the principle of relationship itself — the principle that any two things can stand in proportion to each other, and that some proportions are more fundamental than others. The most fundamental proportion — after identity (1:1) and doubling (2:1) — is 3:2. This is not a human judgment. It is an arithmetic fact.

If an alien civilization transmits a signal meant to be recognized as "musical" — as the Voyager golden record attempted, though it was hampered by its designers' Western biases — the most universally recognizable feature of that signal will be the 3:2 ratio, appearing simultaneously in its pitch structure and its rhythmic structure. The aliens may not have ears. They may not have strings. But if they have wave phenomena — and every physical universe we can imagine has wave phenomena — they will have 3:2.

## Coda

The Sanskrit word for the fifth is पञ्चम (pañcama), "the fifth." The Chinese term is 宮商角徵羽, and the fifth degree is 羽 (yǔ, "feather"). The Greek is διά πασῶν (dia pasōn, "through all"). The Arabic is الخامس (al-khāmis, "the fifth"). Every tradition names it. Every tradition puts it at the center. Every tradition discovers that it cannot be moved.

अचल. Immovable.

The Pythagoreans heard it in a monochord. The ṛṣi heard it in a drone. Ling Lun heard it in a bamboo pipe. An Ewe drummer feels it in her hands. A child feels it when she claps three times in the space of two. An alien will feel it — or whatever its sensory analogue of "feeling" is — when it first allows two periodicities to interfere.

3/2 was not invented. It was there before there was anyone to hear it, and it will be there after the last ear is gone. It is the second-simplest proportion in the universe. And it is enough.
